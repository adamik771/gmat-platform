# Verbal Strategy Guide

## How to Use This Guide

This is a tactical reference, not a lesson. Keep it open while you drill CR and RC. When you miss a question, find the relevant section, re-read the recipe, and run it again on the question you just blew. The recipes are compressed on purpose — every line is something I ran through hundreds of problems on my way to 735.

Pair this with Module 4 (Verbal Precision), which teaches the underlying logic: argument structure, conclusion-spotting, and passage mapping. The lessons build the muscle; this guide is the playbook you reach for when the muscle is there but you need the right move at the right moment.

GMAT Focus Verbal is 23 questions in 45 minutes. Only two question types exist: Critical Reasoning (roughly 11 questions) and Reading Comprehension (roughly 12 questions, grouped by passage). Sentence Correction is gone. Everything in this guide is built for the Focus test — no legacy SC content.

This guide will not replace practice. Read a section, drill 10 questions of that type, come back, re-read. That loop is how it compounds.

## Critical Reasoning: The 9 Question Types

Before anything else: read the question stem first. The stem tells you what kind of answer to hunt for. Reading the argument without knowing the task is how you waste 30 seconds re-reading.

Every CR argument has the same skeleton: **premises** (facts you accept) plus an **assumption** (unstated link) plus a **conclusion** (what the author claims). The assumption is where the action is. Almost every CR type rewards finding it.

### Strengthen

**What you're looking for:** a new fact that, if true, makes the conclusion more likely to follow from the premises. Not a fact that proves the conclusion — just one that tips the scale toward it.

**Key phrase to memorize:** "Which of the following, if true, most supports the argument?" The "if true" matters. You're not evaluating whether the answer is realistic — you're evaluating its effect on the argument assuming it's true.

**Process:**
1. Find the conclusion. Usually preceded by "therefore," "thus," "so," "clearly," or it's the last sentence.
2. Identify the assumption. Ask: what has to be true for these premises to lead to this conclusion?
3. Pick the answer that reinforces the assumption or adds supporting evidence closing the gap.

**Trap patterns to avoid:**
- Answers that restate a premise (no new information — they don't strengthen anything).
- Answers that support a slightly different conclusion than the one stated.
- Answers that are true-sounding but scope-mismatched (right topic, wrong piece of the argument).
- Answers that would strengthen only under additional assumptions the passage doesn't give you.

**One-line summary:** the gap in the argument is [assumption]; find the answer that closes it.

**Worked pattern:** if the argument says "Company X launched product A and profits rose, so A caused the profit rise," a strengthener eliminates alternative causes (nothing else changed in the same quarter) or confirms the causal mechanism (customers specifically bought A). A weakener would instead surface a confound — say, a competitor exiting the market. Learn to flip between these poles quickly; most causal CR questions live in this territory.

### Weaken

**What you're looking for:** a fact that, if true, makes the conclusion less likely. Again: doesn't have to destroy the argument, just tip it the other way.

**Trap:** attacking a premise is almost never the right move. On GMAT CR, premises are treated as true. If the answer choice contradicts a stated premise, it's wrong. Better targets: attack the link between premise and conclusion, introduce an alternative explanation for the evidence, or show the author's assumption is false.

**Process:**
1. Find the conclusion.
2. Identify the assumption the argument needs.
3. Pick the answer that undermines the assumption — or proposes a rival cause, rival explanation, or counterexample that weakens the inference.

**Traps:**
- "Not quite"-weakening answers: they sound negative but don't actually hit the conclusion.
- Out-of-scope answers about a topic the passage doesn't rely on.
- Reverses-direction: strengthens when you wanted to weaken. This happens more than people admit under time pressure.
- Extreme answers that overshoot. You don't need to disprove the argument; you need to weaken it.

### Assumption

**What you're looking for:** something that MUST be true for the argument to hold. Not something that would be nice to have, not something that strengthens — something without which the argument falls apart.

**The negation test:** take the answer choice and negate it. If the argument collapses under the negated version, that's your assumption. If the argument still works, it wasn't a necessary assumption.

**Process:**
1. Identify conclusion and premises.
2. Ask: what gap sits between the premises and the conclusion?
3. For each answer, mentally negate it and ask whether the conclusion still holds.

**Traps:**
- Answers that strengthen but aren't required. "A is beneficial" is rarely an assumption; "A is possible" often is.
- Answers that go too far. Assumptions are typically narrow and minimal, not grand claims.
- Answers about surrounding context rather than the premise-to-conclusion link.

Assumption questions reward the tightest, most minimal answer. When two choices both seem necessary, pick the one closer to the logical skeleton of the argument, not the one adding color.

**The "defender" vs "supporter" split:** some assumptions defend the argument against a specific attack (ruling out an alternative explanation); others supply a positive link the argument needed. Both are legitimate assumption answers. If you've narrowed to two and can't decide, negate each one — whichever negation deals more damage to the conclusion is your answer.

### Inference / Must Be True

**What you're looking for:** a statement that MUST be true given the passage. Not "could be true," not "is probably true," not "seems consistent." Must be true.

**Key distinction from Strengthen/Weaken:** inference goes from passage to conclusion, not the other way. You're the one drawing the conclusion from the stated facts. Nothing new comes in.

**Process:**
1. Read the passage as a set of facts.
2. For each answer, ask: does the passage logically guarantee this?
3. Eliminate anything that requires outside knowledge, extra assumptions, or extrapolation.

**Traps:**
- Answers that are probably true in the real world but not proven by the passage.
- Answers that exaggerate a passage claim — "many" becomes "most," "some" becomes "all."
- Answers that reverse the direction of a conditional or causal statement.
- Answers that combine two passage facts in a way the passage doesn't sanction.

The bar is high. If you need to squint or add a step, it's wrong.

### Flaw in Reasoning

**What you're looking for:** the specific logical error the argument commits. Answers are usually written as abstract descriptions of the flaw, not restatements of content.

**Classify the flaw type as you read:**
- Correlation/causation (X and Y happen together, author claims X causes Y).
- Sample bias (small, unrepresentative, or self-selected sample).
- Ambiguous terms (a word shifts meaning between premises and conclusion).
- False analogy (two situations compared that aren't actually alike in the relevant way).
- Circular reasoning (the conclusion is packed into a premise).
- Percent vs. absolute number confusion.
- Hasty generalization (one case to a broad claim).

**Process:** name the flaw before looking at answers. Then match your description to an answer choice.

**Traps:**
- Answers that describe a real flaw but not the one in this argument.
- Answers that sound abstract and sophisticated but don't apply.
- Answers that critique a premise instead of the reasoning.

### Evaluate the Argument

**What you're looking for:** a question whose answer would most clarify whether the conclusion holds. The correct choice is a useful question, not a fact.

**Test each answer by running both sides:** if the answer to this question is "yes," does the argument get stronger or weaker? If "no," the opposite? If the two scenarios split the argument's validity, you've found the right evaluation question. If both answers leave the argument unchanged, it's irrelevant.

**Traps:**
- Questions that sound relevant but whose answers don't affect the conclusion.
- Questions about details the argument already stated.
- Questions so broad that any answer could go either way.

### Paradox / Resolve Discrepancy

**What you're looking for:** a fact that explains how two seemingly contradictory pieces of information can both be true. You're not choosing a side; you're reconciling them.

**Process:**
1. Identify the two facts that appear to conflict.
2. Ask: what extra piece of information would make both coexist without contradiction?
3. Test answers by plugging them in — does the paradox dissolve?

**Traps:**
- Answers that explain one side but deepen the contradiction on the other.
- Answers that restate the paradox in different words.
- Answers that introduce irrelevant information.
- Answers that weaken one of the two facts instead of reconciling them (remember: both are treated as true).

The correct answer often introduces a third variable — a distinction, a subgroup, a timing difference — that both original facts quietly depended on.

### Boldface

**What you're looking for:** the logical role each bolded phrase plays in the argument.

**Common roles:**
- **Premise / evidence:** supports a conclusion.
- **Conclusion:** the author's main claim.
- **Intermediate conclusion:** supported by some premises, and itself supports the main conclusion.
- **Counterpoint / opposing view:** a position the author disagrees with.
- **Consideration the author dismisses:** acknowledged then set aside.

**Process:**
1. Find the main conclusion first.
2. For each bolded phrase, ask: does the author agree with this? Does it support or oppose the main conclusion? Is it itself supported by other sentences?
3. Match your labels to the answer choices.

**Traps:**
- Answers that get one role right but the other wrong.
- Answers using words like "the main conclusion" when the phrase is actually an intermediate conclusion.
- Answers that describe a role that isn't actually present in the argument.

Eliminate aggressively. If even half of the answer is wrong, the whole answer is wrong.

### Complete the Argument

**What you're looking for:** the conclusion that logically follows from the premises given. The passage ends with a blank or "therefore ___" and you fill it in.

**Process:**
1. Treat it like an inference question. What do the premises, taken together, force you to conclude?
2. The correct answer is tightly supported by the premises, not an extrapolation.
3. Look for answers that complete the logical thread the passage was already pulling on — pay attention to the last sentence's connective ("however," "therefore," "yet").

**Traps:**
- Answers that are broader than the premises support.
- Answers that go in a direction the passage wasn't building toward.
- Answers that restate a premise instead of concluding from them.

## CR Trap Patterns (The 7 You Must Recognize)

Trap answer choices are recycled. Once you know the patterns, you spot them reflexively and save seconds on every question.

**1. Out of scope.** The answer talks about something the argument never mentioned. It might be true in the real world and even feel relevant, but if the premises don't touch the topic, the answer can't strengthen, weaken, or be assumed. Quick test: can I point to a word or concept in the passage this answer is responding to? If no — cut it.

**2. Wrong direction.** You needed to weaken; the answer strengthens. Or vice versa. This trap wrecks people under time pressure because the answer is on-topic and logically connected — it's just pointing the wrong way. Always re-read the question stem before committing. The stem is where direction is set.

**3. Absolute language.** Words like "always," "never," "only," "all," "none," "must" are usually wrong on CR — especially in assumption and inference questions. Real arguments rarely require absolutes. When you see an absolute word, ask: does the passage actually guarantee this strength of claim? Usually it doesn't.

**4. Reverses causality.** Argument says A causes B; the answer treats B as causing A, or has them both caused by a third thing. This shows up most on strengthen/weaken for causal arguments. When evaluating a causal claim, always check: could this answer be rearranging the direction of cause?

**5. Attacks the wrong part.** The argument's weakness is in the leap from premise to conclusion, but the answer attacks a premise instead. Premises are treated as true, so attacking them is off-target. Keep your focus on the inference, not the inputs.

**6. Too narrow (or too broad).** The correct answer matches the scope of the conclusion. If the conclusion is about all CEOs and the answer is about one CEO at one company, it's too narrow. If the conclusion is about one specific policy and the answer is about economic policy in general, it's too broad. Match scope.

**7. Plausible but unsupported.** The answer sounds reasonable and fits a real-world pattern, but nothing in the passage backs it. This is the most seductive trap because nothing feels wrong — until you ask "where does the passage say this?" and can't point to a line. Always demand textual support.

When you're stuck between two answers, name which of these traps might be hiding in each. Nine times out of ten, the trap label eliminates one of them.

## Reading Comprehension: The Structure Map

RC rewards structure, not retention. You're not trying to memorize the passage — you're trying to map it so you can navigate back when questions ask for detail.

**The 4-element map to build while reading:**

**1. Main point.** What is the whole passage arguing or explaining? Summarize it in one sentence in your head by the end of the passage. If you can't, you didn't read it actively enough. Main point questions hit roughly one out of every four RC sets, so this is always paying rent.

**2. Structure.** Which template is the passage following?
- Problem-solution: here's an issue, here's how it's being addressed.
- Compare-contrast: two views, two eras, two approaches.
- Chronological: evolution of an idea or phenomenon over time.
- Argumentative: author stakes a position and defends it against alternatives.
- Expository: explains a concept or phenomenon without taking a side.

**3. Author's attitude.** What's the author's stance — neutral, critical, supportive, skeptical, uncertain, admiring, cautious? Attitude questions appear often. Build the answer into your map instead of hunting for it later.

**4. Paragraph purposes.** What does each paragraph DO? Not what it's about — what it's doing. Introducing a view? Refuting it? Providing evidence? Qualifying an earlier claim? Offering a counterexample? Drawing a conclusion?

**Time budget:** 2-3 minutes max on the first read. If you're still reading at 3 minutes, move to questions — you'll go back anyway. Perfectionism on the first read kills your clock.

**The "active reading" habit:** pause at the end of each paragraph. Mentally label its role in one short phrase: "sets up the debate," "lists problems with view X," "author's alternative." This forces you to process as you go. Without this pause, your eyes move but your brain doesn't.

Do not underline, do not highlight, do not take notes during RC. The test interface is clunky and notes cost time. The structure map lives in your head.

## RC Question Types: Tactical Recipes

**Main Idea / Primary Purpose.** The answer must cover the WHOLE passage, not just one paragraph. A trap answer describes paragraph 2 accurately — but the correct answer describes what the passage as a whole is doing. Process: restate the main point in your own words before looking at answers. Match your version to the closest choice. Eliminate answers that are too narrow (one paragraph only), too broad (beyond the passage's claim), or wrong in tone (call a neutral passage "critical"). The correct answer often starts with a verb: "to argue," "to explain," "to compare."

**Specific Detail.** Always go back to the passage and verify. Never trust memory — GMAT writers set traps specifically for people who skip this step. Process: find the keyword from the question stem in the passage, read the surrounding 2-3 sentences, and pick the answer that matches. Traps: answers that use passage words in a different relationship than the passage states; answers that combine two facts from different paragraphs; answers that paraphrase a claim more extremely than the passage did.

**Inference.** What MUST be true based on the passage. Same bar as CR inference — not "probably," not "likely." Process: find the relevant section, identify what the passage actually says, and pick the answer that follows necessarily. Traps: answers that introduce real-world knowledge not in the passage; answers that over-generalize from a single example; answers that flip the direction of a conditional.

**Author's Attitude / Tone.** Look for evaluative words in the passage: "unfortunately," "surprisingly," "nevertheless," "remarkably," "problematic." These are the author's fingerprints. Traps: answers that are too extreme (calling mild skepticism "harsh dismissal") or too neutral (calling clear advocacy "objective description"). Match the intensity as well as the direction. When in doubt, moderate answers beat extreme ones.

**Function.** Why is this sentence or paragraph here? What role does it play? Process: look at the sentence/paragraph in context — what comes before it, what comes after? Is it supporting a claim, introducing a counterexample, qualifying an earlier point, transitioning between ideas? The answer will usually describe a logical role, not a topic. Traps: answers that describe what the sentence says instead of what it's doing.

**Application.** Which hypothetical scenario aligns with the author's position (or, less often, contradicts it)? Process: identify the principle or view the author endorses, then test each scenario against it. Which hypothetical would the author see as an instance of their principle in action? Traps: scenarios that share surface features with the passage but violate the underlying principle.

**Across all types:** if two answers survive your first cut, the correct answer is almost always the one more tightly tied to specific passage language. The trap is usually the one that sounds more sophisticated.

## Passage Type Recognition

You can predict a lot about a passage from its topic. Use the first paragraph to classify, then read with the right expectations.

**Social / behavioral science.** Usually argument-based. The author has a thesis — often defending a newer view against a traditional one, or rejecting an oversimplified account. Watch for stance markers. Attitude questions are common. Expect specific detail questions that test whether you noticed qualifying language.

**Humanities / literary criticism.** Often critical of received wisdom. The author is pushing back against a standard reading or a previous generation of critics. The passage will usually introduce a traditional view, poke holes in it, and offer an alternative. Function questions are common here — a lot of paragraphs are doing the job of setting up views to knock down.

**Natural science.** Usually expository, not argumentative. The author is explaining a phenomenon, a mechanism, or a finding. Attitude is neutral in the vast majority of cases. Detail and inference questions dominate. The difficulty lives in the density of information, not in tracking a debate.

**Business / management.** Often case-study format or framework exposition. The passage may describe a problem firms face and a strategy for addressing it. Inference and application questions are common — "which company would best exemplify this approach?"

**Historical analysis.** Argumentative. The author is making a thesis about what really caused something, what a period really represented, what a figure's real contribution was. Expect main idea questions that test whether you identified the thesis, and function questions about why the author brought up this or that example.

Classifying the passage takes ten seconds and changes how you read. Do it.

## Pacing Strategy for Verbal (45 min, 23 Qs)

**Average:** roughly 1:57 per question. But the average is a lie — CR and RC run on different clocks.

**CR target:** 1:30 to 2:00 per question. Easy ones should close in 1:15. Hard ones can justify 2:15 if you're actually making progress. Anything beyond that and you're losing time you can't get back.

**RC target:** 2:30 to 3:00 to read and map the passage, then roughly 1:00 per question. A 4-question passage should close in around 6:30-7:00.

**When to skip:**
- CR: if you're stuck after 90 seconds and haven't narrowed to two choices, flag it, guess, move on. You can bookmark and review. Don't sit on a single question for 3+ minutes — you're not thinking, you're stalling.
- RC: don't skip an entire passage (you already paid the reading cost). But within a passage set, do the easier questions first, leave the hardest for last, and come back if you have time.

**Buffer:** aim to finish with 2-3 minutes remaining. Use that buffer to revisit flagged questions, not to second-guess ones you already answered with confidence.

**Pacing checkpoints:**
- After 15 minutes: you should be around question 8.
- After 30 minutes: around question 15-16.
- At 40 minutes: you should be on your last passage or last CR cluster.

If you're behind, don't sprint to catch up on one hard question — skip and move. If you're ahead, don't slow down luxuriously; use the buffer for review.

## Common Verbal Failure Modes

These are the habits I see wreck Verbal scores. Most have nothing to do with content knowledge.

**Re-reading every answer choice.** You read A-E, then go back and read A again, then B again. This doubles your per-question time. Commit to a first-pass filter: on the first read, eliminate anything clearly wrong, mark anything plausible, then decide between the survivors.

**Falling in love with the first good-looking answer.** Answer B looks great, you pick it, you move on — and you skipped a better D. The fix: even when A or B looks right, scan C, D, E quickly before committing. On CR especially, the trap is often choice B; the correct answer is often D or E.

**Spending too long when 50/50 between two answers.** If you've eliminated three and can't decide between two, you're at the point of diminishing returns. Ninety seconds of extra staring rarely breaks the tie. Pick the one more tightly anchored to the passage, commit, move on. Stewing costs more than being wrong.

**Getting emotionally invested in a hard passage.** The passage is dense, the questions feel unfair, you decide you're going to conquer it. Meanwhile the clock burns. The test doesn't reward heroism — it rewards finishing. Move on from a bad passage; don't let it take down the next one too.

**Changing correct answers on second-guess.** Statistically, first-instinct answers are right more often than changed answers. Only change an answer if you spot a specific error in your reasoning, not because you got nervous.

**Over-relying on vocabulary.** GMAT Verbal is a logic test dressed up in business-school English. Knowing a rare word rarely helps. Knowing argument structure always does. If you find yourself picking answers because they "sound smart," recalibrate.

## Non-Native Speaker Notes

See Module 8 for the full playbook — this is the short version.

You're not at a disadvantage on GMAT Focus Verbal because it's a logic test, not an English test. Native speakers flunk these questions at the same rate non-natives do when both have equal logical training. I scored 735 as a non-native speaker.

**Short list:**
- **Idiom flashcards are mostly irrelevant now.** With SC gone, the long idiom lists from old prep books are low-leverage. Don't waste study time on them.
- **Read daily in English.** The Economist, NYT, FT, Atlantic. Not for vocabulary — for structure. Read editorials and op-eds and try to identify the main conclusion and assumptions while you read. That is CR practice disguised as leisure.
- **Drill CR before RC.** CR is shorter and teaches you the logical vocabulary you'll then use on RC. Build the CR muscle first.
- **Build speed on RC with dense non-fiction.** Long-form articles from The Economist and The Atlantic train your eye to extract structure from complex prose. If you can map a 1,200-word Economist piece in two minutes, an RC passage will feel short.
- **Don't translate.** If you're converting English to your native language and back, you're spending 20% of your clock on translation. Train yourself to read, think, and answer in English only.
