# Verbal Strategy Guide

GMAT Focus Verbal: **23 questions in 45 minutes (~1:57 per question)**. No Sentence Correction. Two question types: Critical Reasoning (~12) and Reading Comprehension (~11, grouped by passage).

---

## Part 1: Critical Reasoning Framework

### Step 1: Identify the Question Type Before Reading the Argument
CR question stems tell you what to do. Read them first. Common types:
- **Strengthen:** "Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?"
- **Weaken:** "Which of the following, if true, most weakens the argument?"
- **Assumption:** "The argument depends on which of the following assumptions?"
- **Inference:** "Which of the following can be inferred from the passage?"
- **Evaluate:** "Which of the following would be most useful to know in evaluating the argument?"
- **Flaw:** "The argument is flawed because..."
- **Paradox/Discrepancy:** "Which of the following best explains the apparent contradiction?"

Knowing the type tells you what to look for while reading.

### Step 2: Read the Argument
Identify:
1. **Premise(s):** What facts does the argument give you?
2. **Conclusion:** What claim does the author make?
3. **Assumption (implicit):** What must be true to connect the premises to the conclusion?

### Step 3: Before Looking at Answer Choices, Predict Your Answer
- Strengthen: What would make the conclusion more likely?
- Weaken: What would make the conclusion less likely?
- Assumption: What unstated belief must be true?

Even a rough prediction helps you eliminate wrong answers faster.

### Step 4: Evaluate Each Answer Choice
- Eliminate answers that are **irrelevant** (don't affect the conclusion)
- Eliminate answers that **shift scope** (talk about something slightly different)
- Eliminate answers that are **too extreme** (use "always," "never," "only")
- Eliminate answers that **reverse the logic** (strengthen when you need weaken, etc.)

### Step 5: Final Check
Re-read the question stem to make sure you're answering the right question. Many wrong answers would be correct for a DIFFERENT question type.

## Question Type Playbooks

### Strengthen Questions
**Goal:** Find the answer that adds support to the conclusion.

**Tactics:**
- Look for answers that support the underlying assumption
- Look for answers that eliminate alternative explanations
- "If true" means: assume it's true, then ask how it affects the argument
- The best strengthener doesn't need to prove the conclusion — just make it more likely

**Trap answers:**
- Answers that support a PREMISE but not the conclusion
- Answers that are consistent with the argument but don't strengthen it
- Answers that are too extreme to be supported

### Weaken Questions
**Goal:** Find the answer that undermines the conclusion.

**Tactics:**
- Look for answers that attack the assumption
- Look for answers that provide an alternative explanation
- Look for answers that reveal the data is misleading (wrong sample, confounding variable, etc.)
- The best weakener doesn't need to disprove the conclusion — just make it less likely

**Trap answers:**
- Answers that mention something negative but don't actually affect the conclusion
- Answers that weaken a premise but leave the conclusion intact
- Answers that are outside the scope of the argument

### Assumption Questions
**Goal:** Find the unstated idea that MUST be true for the argument to hold.

**The Negation Test:** Negate the answer choice. If the argument falls apart, it's a necessary assumption. If the argument still works, it's not the assumption.

**Example:**
- Argument: "Smoking rates dropped after the tax. Therefore, higher taxes reduce smoking."
- Candidate assumption: "The price increase from the tax was significant enough to affect consumer behavior."
- Negate: "The price increase was NOT significant enough to affect consumer behavior."
- Effect on argument: the argument collapses (if the price didn't affect behavior, the tax didn't cause the drop). → It's the assumption.

**Trap answers:**
- Answers that strengthen but aren't necessary
- Answers that are outside the scope

### Inference Questions
**Goal:** Find the answer that MUST be true based on the passage.

**Tactics:**
- Stay close to the text — don't add outside knowledge
- The correct answer is usually MODEST, not dramatic
- Look for answers that can be directly supported by a specific line
- "Must be true" is stricter than "might be true" — the answer has to be unambiguously supported

**Trap answers:**
- Answers that go one step too far (use "completely," "always," etc.)
- Answers that require outside assumptions
- Answers that are consistent with the passage but aren't specifically supported

### Evaluate Questions
**Goal:** Find the question whose answer would affect the argument.

**Tactics:**
- Both possible answers to the evaluation question should affect the conclusion (one strengthens, one weakens)
- If the answer to the evaluation question wouldn't change anything, it's not useful

**Example:**
- Argument: "Company X is profitable because its marketing works."
- Good evaluation: "What percentage of new customers come from the marketing campaign?"
- If the answer is "90%," the argument is strengthened. If "5%," weakened. Either way, it helps evaluate.

### Flaw Questions
**Goal:** Identify the logical error in the argument.

**Common flaws:**
- **Correlation vs causation:** Assuming A causes B just because they occur together
- **Sample bias:** Assuming a non-representative group represents a larger population
- **Ignoring alternative explanations:** Treating one cause as the only possible cause
- **Circular reasoning:** Using the conclusion as a premise
- **Ad hominem:** Attacking the person instead of the argument
- **Appeal to authority:** Citing an expert whose authority doesn't apply

### Paradox / Discrepancy Questions
**Goal:** Find the answer that RESOLVES the apparent contradiction.

**Tactics:**
- Identify the contradiction clearly
- Look for an answer that explains how both conflicting facts can be true simultaneously
- The answer usually introduces new information that reconciles the two sides

## Part 2: Reading Comprehension Framework

### Step 1: Preview the Questions (20-30 seconds)
Skim the question stems before reading the passage. This tells you what to look for.

### Step 2: Read the Passage Actively (2-3 minutes)
Don't read like a novel. Use this framework:

**For each paragraph:**
1. Read the first sentence carefully — usually contains the paragraph's main idea
2. Skim the middle — note where details and examples are, but don't memorize them
3. Read the last sentence — often introduces a conclusion or transition

**After reading:**
- Summarize the passage in one sentence: "This passage argues _____ and supports it with _____."
- Note the author's tone: objective, critical, enthusiastic, skeptical, etc.

### Step 3: Answer Each Question
1. Read the question carefully
2. Try to answer in your own words before looking at choices
3. Find evidence in the passage to support your answer
4. Eliminate choices that lack support, are too extreme, or are off-topic

## RC Question Types

### Main Idea Questions
**Tactics:**
- The correct answer covers the WHOLE passage, not just one paragraph
- Eliminate answers that describe only one section
- Eliminate answers that are too broad or too narrow

### Specific Detail Questions
**Tactics:**
- Go back to the passage and find the exact line
- The correct answer is usually a paraphrase, not a direct quote
- Trap: answers that use words from the passage but change the meaning

### Inference Questions (RC)
**Tactics:**
- The correct answer must be supported by the text
- Correct inferences are usually modest — "is consistent with," "suggests that"
- Trap: answers that go one step too far beyond what's stated

### Application Questions
**Tactics:**
- Think about the author's perspective and tone
- Look for consistency with the author's stated views
- Trap: answers the author might agree with generally but that aren't supported by this specific passage

### Function / Structure Questions
**Tactics:**
- Ask: "What role does this sentence/paragraph play in the overall argument?"
- Common roles: introducing an example, providing evidence, offering a counterpoint, restating a conclusion

## Common Verbal Traps (Applies to Both CR and RC)

### 1. The Scope Shift
The argument talks about "some employees" but the answer choice talks about "all employees." Watch for: some → all, one → every, short-term → long-term, correlation → causation.

### 2. The Irrelevant Authority
"Studies have shown..." — sounds authoritative but isn't specifically about the argument's topic. Relevance matters more than credibility.

### 3. The Emotional Appeal
An answer that feels right morally but doesn't logically connect to the argument. GMAT rewards logic, not values.

### 4. The Partial Truth
An answer that is mostly correct but has one wrong clause. Read every word. A single wrong element invalidates the whole choice.

### 5. The Extreme Answer
Words like "always," "never," "only," "completely" are usually wrong for inference questions. Correct inferences are typically moderate: "is likely," "may contribute," "suggests."

### 6. The Reversal
An answer that uses the right keywords but in the wrong direction (e.g., strengthens when you need to weaken).

### 7. The Out-of-Scope
An answer that is true and reasonable but doesn't address the specific argument. Stay within the scope of what was written.

## Pacing Strategy for Verbal

- **Target:** ~1:57 per question
- **CR:** 1:30-2:00 per question
- **RC:** 2:30-3:00 for reading + 1:00-1:30 per question

If you're stuck for more than 2:30, make your best guess and move on. Never spend more than 3:00 on any single question.

---

## Checklist Before Selecting an Answer

- [ ] Did I identify the question type correctly?
- [ ] Did I predict my answer before reading choices?
- [ ] Did I eliminate obvious wrong answers?
- [ ] Does my choice match the conclusion/inference scope?
- [ ] Am I answering the question that's actually being asked?
- [ ] Are the extreme words ("always," "never") appropriate for this question type?
