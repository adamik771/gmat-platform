# Module 2: Diagnostic Deep Dive

## Learning Objectives
- Take a meaningful diagnostic and interpret every data point
- Build your error log from Day 1
- Distinguish between content gaps and execution gaps
- Understand why mock scores lie and how to read them correctly

---

## How to Take a Diagnostic Properly

A diagnostic is useless if taken carelessly. The goal is to get an honest measurement — not to impress yourself.

### Rules for Your Diagnostic

1. **Simulate real conditions.** Find a quiet room. Set a timer. No phone. No breaks beyond what the real test allows (one 10-minute break).
2. **Don't look up anything.** If you don't know a concept, guess and move on. The point is to expose gaps, not hide them.
3. **Track your time per question.** If your test platform doesn't do this, note which questions you rushed on.
4. **Don't panic about the score.** This is your starting point, not your ending point. A 550 diagnostic is normal — even students who eventually score 750+ often start there.

---

## Interpreting Your Results

After your diagnostic, you have three types of data:

### 1. Section Scores
Your Quant, Verbal, and DI scores tell you the macro picture. Example:
- Q78 / V79 / DI77 → relatively balanced but all need work
- Q85 / V72 / DI80 → Verbal is the clear priority

**Action:** Allocate study time proportionally to your weakest section, but never completely ignore any section.

### 2. Topic-Level Accuracy
Most platforms break down accuracy by topic. This is where real planning starts.

Look for:
- **Topics below 50% accuracy**: These are content gaps. You need to study the underlying concepts.
- **Topics at 50-70% accuracy**: These are mixed. You may know the concept but make execution errors.
- **Topics above 80% accuracy**: These are strengths. Maintain them with occasional practice but don't over-invest.

### 3. Time Data
Look at how long you spent per question:
- GMAT Focus gives you about **2 minutes per Quant question**, **2 minutes per Verbal question**, and **2 minutes 15 seconds per DI question**
- If you're consistently going over 3 minutes, that's a pacing issue — not just a content issue

## Content Gaps vs. Execution Gaps

This distinction is critical because the fix is completely different.

### Content Gap
**Symptom:** You look at a question and don't know where to start. The concept is unfamiliar.
**Example:** You see a probability question involving combinations and don't know the formula.
**Fix:** Go back to fundamentals. Watch/read the lesson. Do 15-20 problems from easy to hard. Build understanding from the ground up.

### Execution Gap
**Symptom:** You know the concept, but you get the question wrong anyway — either through a careless mistake, running out of time, or using an inefficient method.
**Example:** You know how to solve a rate problem but set up the equation with the wrong units and get a wrong answer.
**Fix:** Practice under timed conditions. Build checklists for common errors. Review mistake patterns.

### How to Tell the Difference
Ask yourself: "If I had unlimited time and could look up any formula, would I get this right?"
- If **yes** → execution gap
- If **no** → content gap

Your error log should tag each mistake as one or the other. Over time, you'll see which type dominates — and that tells you whether to study more content or practice more execution.

## Building Your Study Plan from the Diagnostic

### Step 1: Rank Your Weak Topics
List every topic where your accuracy was below 70%. Rank them by:
1. How frequently the topic appears on the GMAT (high-frequency topics first)
2. How large the gap is (lower accuracy = higher priority)

### Step 2: Block Your Calendar
Decide how many hours per day you can realistically study. Then allocate:
- **60% to weak topics** (content review + practice)
- **25% to medium topics** (practice + error review)
- **15% to strengths** (maintenance + mixed practice sets)

### Step 3: Set Milestone Checkpoints
- **Week 2:** Take a short section-specific quiz. Did your weakest topic improve?
- **Week 4:** Take a full practice test. Compare to diagnostic.
- **Every 2-3 weeks after:** Take another practice test and adjust the plan.

### Step 4: Adjust Based on Data
Your plan should change every 2-3 weeks based on new data. A topic that was weak in Week 1 might be solid by Week 4. Shift your focus accordingly.

## Why Your Mock Scores Lie (and How to Read Them)

Mock test scores fluctuate. A lot. Here's what most people don't understand:

### Normal Variance
A GMAT practice test has a margin of error of roughly ±30 points. That means:
- If your "true" level is 680, individual tests might range from 650 to 710
- A single test score tells you very little
- The **trend over 4-5 tests** tells you everything

### What Inflates Mock Scores
- Taking the same mock twice (you remember questions)
- Testing in a comfortable environment (your desk vs. a test center)
- No real stakes (less anxiety = better performance)

### What Deflates Mock Scores
- Testing when tired or distracted
- Taking a mock too soon after intensive study (fatigue)
- Encountering a particularly hard set of questions

### How to Read Mocks Correctly
- Look at the **average of your last 3-4 mocks**, not any single score
- Look at **section-level trends**, not just the total
- If you're consistently scoring 680-710, your actual test will likely fall in that range (possibly slightly lower due to test-day pressure)

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Don't skip the diagnostic and "just start studying."** Without a baseline, you can't measure progress or allocate time effectively.
2. **Don't take a diagnostic and then ignore the data.** The value is in the analysis, not the number.
3. **Don't retake the diagnostic hoping for a better score.** You need an honest baseline.
4. **Don't compare your diagnostic to your target and feel overwhelmed.** A 150+ point improvement is completely achievable with structured prep over 3-6 months.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Your diagnostic is a roadmap, not a verdict
- Separate content gaps from execution gaps — they require different fixes
- Allocate study time based on data, not feelings
- Mock scores have a ±30 point variance — track trends, not individual scores
- Revisit and adjust your plan every 2-3 weeks
