# Module 5: Data Insights

## Why Data Insights Is the Differentiator

Here is the single most underappreciated fact about GMAT Focus: Data Insights is where the score race is actually decided. Quant is saturated — every student who walked into a GMAT classroom in the last decade has drilled Problem Solving. Verbal is saturated — Reading Comprehension and Critical Reasoning have been around forever. But Data Insights is new. It was introduced in late 2023 when GMAT Focus replaced the classic GMAT, and most candidates still treat it as an afterthought. They cram Quant, they grind Verbal, and then two weeks before test day they glance at DI and think, "I'll wing the rest."

That is a scoring catastrophe. On my own 735, the DI section is what separated the top-percentile total from a very good total. I can tell you with full confidence, from reviewing student score reports across hundreds of sittings: a well-prepared DI score is worth between **20 and 40 total points** over a candidate who did the same Quant and Verbal prep but neglected DI. That is enormous. That is the difference between Harvard and a school you did not want to mention on your application.

Why is it so gameable? Three reasons. First, the five question formats are rigidly consistent — once you know the rules of Data Sufficiency, every single DS question you ever see uses the same five answer choices in the same order. The same rigidity applies to Table Analysis, Graphics Interpretation, Multi-Source Reasoning, and Two-Part Analysis. Format knowledge compounds. Second, the test writers are still early in the Focus era, which means the trap surfaces are narrower and more predictable than on Quant or Verbal. Third, students do not drill DI enough to hit the ceiling on format-specific technique, so even a month of targeted practice puts you ahead of most of your cohort.

The philosophical point: DI is a hybrid section. It tests quantitative reasoning, reading comprehension, and data literacy in one 45-minute block. That means if you are strong in Quant and Verbal, DI should be your best score — but only if you have trained the format. Do not skip this module. I spend more time with students on DI per hour of improvement than on any other section, because the return is highest.

## The DI Landscape

The bare numbers: **20 questions in 45 minutes**. That is 2 minutes 15 seconds per question on average, which sounds generous until you realize some formats take far longer than others. The section is question-level adaptive, meaning your next question is selected based on your running performance — so early questions carry disproportionate weight in calibrating your difficulty band. Do not rush the first six questions.

The five formats, and rough distribution:

- **Data Sufficiency (DS)** — 25 to 30% of the section. 5 to 6 questions. Standalone questions, same five answer choices every time.
- **Multi-Source Reasoning (MSR)** — usually 1 set of 3 questions tied to 3 tabs of source material. 3 questions total.
- **Table Analysis (TA)** — 3 to 4 questions, each anchored to a sortable table. Typically each question has three true/false sub-statements.
- **Graphics Interpretation (GI)** — 3 to 4 questions, each with a chart or graph plus two fill-in-the-blank dropdowns.
- **Two-Part Analysis (TPA)** — 3 to 4 questions, each with a two-column answer grid.

You do not control the order. The section shuffles formats, so a DS question can be followed by an MSR set, then a GI, then another DS, then a TA. This matters for your pacing: you cannot budget a fixed "MSR block" in your head. You have to be fluent in switching formats on the fly.

**Critical fact that most students miss: you get an on-screen calculator in DI, and only in DI.** Quant does not have one. Verbal does not need one. DI does. It is a basic four-function calculator with memory. The calculator is a trap as much as a tool — more on that in the Failure Modes section — but knowing it exists changes how you approach arithmetic-heavy questions. You can afford to compute 17.6% of 2,340 exactly when it matters. You cannot afford to break out the calculator for every single arithmetic step, because the 2:15 clock punishes that habit.

Two more landscape details worth internalizing. First, DI scoring is on the same 60-90 scale as Quant and Verbal on GMAT Focus, and it contributes equally to your total 205-805 score. A weak DI pulls down your total one-for-one. Second, you cannot skip questions and return — GMAT Focus allows you to bookmark and review up to three questions per section within the section, but you must answer in order to advance. Plan your skips carefully.

## Format 1: Data Sufficiency (DS)

DS is the heart of DI. Roughly 5 to 6 of your 20 questions will be DS, and it is the most technique-intensive format in the entire GMAT. It is also the format students get most wrong because they misunderstand the meta-question.

**The meta-question is: "Do I have enough information to answer?" It is NOT "What is the answer?"**

Every DS problem presents a question prompt followed by two statements, labeled (1) and (2). Your job is to decide whether statement (1) alone, statement (2) alone, both together, or neither is sufficient to produce a unique answer to the question. You do not need to compute the answer. You just need to know whether you could.

### The five answer choices — memorize them cold

These five choices appear on every DS question in this exact order. You should be able to recite them in your sleep:

- **(A)** Statement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
- **(B)** Statement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
- **(C)** BOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
- **(D)** EACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
- **(E)** Statements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.

The elimination flow: check statement (1) alone first. If sufficient, the answer is A or D. If not sufficient, the answer is B, C, or E. Then check statement (2) alone. That narrows you to a single letter, except in the "neither alone" branch, where you then check them together to decide between C and E.

### The three most common DS traps

**Trap 1: Assuming integers when the question doesn't say so.** If the problem says "x is a positive number" and does not say "integer," then x can be 0.5 or 2.7 or any real number. Students default to integer thinking and get burned. Always ask: what kind of number is this? Integer? Rational? Positive? Nonzero? The stem either tells you or it doesn't — and when it doesn't, you must consider non-integers.

**Trap 2: Combining statements too quickly without checking each alone.** Many students look at both statements, see they combine nicely, and pick C. But sometimes statement (1) was already sufficient on its own. Always evaluate each statement in isolation before combining. If you jump to combining, you miss answer A or B or D.

**Trap 3: Answering the wrong question.** The stem might ask "What is the value of x + y?" You find x and y individually and feel triumphant. But did you check whether you could find the sum without finding each separately? Often statements give you the sum directly, and the individual values are a distraction. Re-read the exact question before evaluating sufficiency.

### Worked example 1 — quantitative

> **Question:** What is the value of x?
> **(1)** 2x + 4 = 10
> **(2)** x² = 9

Statement (1): 2x + 4 = 10 gives x = 3. One unique value. **Sufficient.** So answer is A or D.

Statement (2): x² = 9 gives x = 3 or x = −3. Two possible values. **Not sufficient** — no unique answer.

Since (1) is sufficient and (2) is not, the answer is **(A)**.

Notice the trap here. A careless student might look at statement (2) and think, "x = 3, done." That is the integer/positive assumption trap. x² = 9 has two solutions, and without additional info, you cannot pick one. Sufficiency requires a **unique** answer.

### Worked example 2 — yes/no question

> **Question:** Is x > 0?
> **(1)** x² > 0
> **(2)** x³ > 0

This is a yes/no DS question. Sufficiency means you can answer either "definitely yes" or "definitely no." "It depends" is not sufficient.

Statement (1): x² > 0 means x ≠ 0, but x could be positive (like 2) or negative (like −2). Answer could be yes or no. **Not sufficient.**

Statement (2): x³ > 0. Cubes preserve sign. If x³ is positive, x must be positive. **Sufficient** — the answer is definitely "yes."

Since (1) is not sufficient and (2) is sufficient, the answer is **(B)**.

The key move: for yes/no questions, sufficiency means the answer is consistently yes or consistently no. If you can construct one case saying yes and another saying no from the same statement, the statement is not sufficient.

### The "C trap"

This is the DS trap students fall into most often at the 80+ scoring band. Both statements together look sufficient, both statements alone look insufficient, and the student picks C. But often one statement alone was actually sufficient — they just did not think hard enough about it. **Answer D**, not C.

The fix: before you combine, force yourself to spend at least 15 seconds on each statement alone, trying to find counterexamples. If you cannot find a counterexample, the statement is sufficient. Most C-trap errors come from giving up on individual evaluation too early.

A related trap in the other direction: both statements together look sufficient to a hurried student, but actually they are not — there is still ambiguity even with both. That is answer E. The remedy is the same: test counterexamples with both statements combined before selecting C.

## Format 2: Multi-Source Reasoning (MSR)

MSR is the longest individual unit of work in the section. You get a source consisting of usually **3 tabs** — some combination of a written passage, a table, a chart, or a list of rules — and then **3 questions** tied to that source. You navigate between tabs by clicking, and you cannot see two tabs simultaneously.

3 questions sharing one source means the up-front reading cost is amortized. That is both a trap and an opportunity.

### Strategy: skim first, build a mental index

Do not read every word on every tab before starting question 1. The reason is simple: you do not yet know which details matter. Reading carefully first means you waste 2 minutes memorizing the color of the CEO's tie, then spend another 2 minutes re-reading when the question asks about quarterly revenue.

Instead: **skim all three tabs in 60 to 90 seconds** to build a mental map. For each tab, note:

- What is on this tab? (Prose? Table? Chart?)
- What topic does it cover? (Revenue? Product specs? Timeline?)
- Where are the main data points or key claims located?

You are building an index, not learning the content. Then when question 1 asks about "the difference in units sold between Q2 and Q4," you know instantly: that's on Tab 2, the table. You flip there, extract the two numbers, compute, answer. Done.

### Worked mini-example

Imagine an MSR set for a fictional company, Lumina Optics, launching a new product:

**Tab 1 (Email):** An internal email from the VP of Product dated March 2026 explaining that Lumina plans to launch the X9 camera in three markets: North America, Europe, and Japan. The email mentions the North American launch is delayed pending regulatory approval.

**Tab 2 (Table):** A forecast table showing projected units sold by region and quarter. North America: 0 in Q1, 40k in Q2, 80k in Q3, 120k in Q4. Europe: 20k, 45k, 70k, 90k. Japan: 10k, 25k, 40k, 55k.

**Tab 3 (Chart):** A bar chart showing total projected revenue by quarter, with each bar split by region.

Now three questions:

**Q1:** Did Lumina project any North American sales in Q1? → Tab 2, row 1. Zero. Easy.

**Q2:** Based on the email and the table, is the delay consistent with the forecast? → Requires Tab 1 (the delay) AND Tab 2 (zero in Q1 North America). Cross-reference. Consistent.

**Q3:** Which region contributes the largest share of Q4 revenue? → Tab 3, Q4 bar. Read the chart.

Notice the pattern: every question pulls from a different tab or combination. Your mental index lets you go directly to the right tab without re-reading the others.

### MSR-specific tips

- **Dates and qualifiers matter.** MSR passages often use exact dates, specific conditions ("if approved by regulators"), and conditional phrasings. A question may hinge on whether a condition was met. Mark these mentally during your skim.
- **Later tabs often modify or qualify earlier tabs.** If tab 1 says "the launch is planned for April" and tab 3 says "the launch was postponed to June," tab 3 wins. Read all three tabs, in order, during your skim.
- **"Can you determine X from the information provided?"** is a common MSR question type. It is asking a sufficiency question — do you have enough data to answer? If any required piece is missing, the answer is "cannot be determined."
- **Do not infer beyond the tabs.** If a tab does not say the product is profitable, do not assume it is. MSR tests strict reading, not business intuition.

Budget: aim for about **7 minutes total** on an MSR set — 90 seconds of skimming plus roughly 110 seconds per question. That averages to 2:20 per question including the shared reading overhead.

## Format 3: Table Analysis (TA)

TA presents a sortable table — typically 8 to 20 rows, 3 to 6 columns — and asks a single question that contains **three true/false sub-statements**. You answer each sub-statement independently. All three must be correct for credit; getting two out of three means zero.

### Strategy: sort by the relevant column, then scan

The table is sortable by clicking column headers. This is not decorative — it is the single most important feature of the TA format. For almost every sub-statement, there is a column you should sort by to make verification trivial.

If the sub-statement is "More than half the countries have GDP per capita above $20,000," sort by GDP per capita descending, count the rows above $20,000, compare to the total. Thirty seconds of work instead of eyeballing unsorted data.

If the sub-statement is "The country with the largest population has the lowest unemployment rate," sort by population descending to find the top row, note its unemployment rate, then sort by unemployment ascending to see if the same country appears at the top. If yes, true. If no, false.

### Common TA traps

- **Forgetting units.** Columns often have subtle unit labels: "Revenue (millions USD)" vs "Revenue growth (%)." A sub-statement about "revenue greater than 100" could mean 100 million or 100% — read the column header, not the cell.
- **Misreading row vs column totals.** Some tables include totals at the bottom or a total column on the right. A sub-statement about "the highest value in the dataset" might refer to individual cells, not the totals row. Check what the sub-statement is asking before you look.
- **Assuming correlation means causation in a T/F setup.** A sub-statement might say "Countries with higher GDP also have lower unemployment." Even if that pattern holds in the data, the T/F is about whether the data *shows* this pattern, not whether it's causally true. Check the pattern, not the causal implication.
- **"All" vs "most" vs "some."** "All countries have unemployment below 10%" is very different from "Most countries have unemployment below 10%." A single counterexample kills an "all" sub-statement. Skim for outliers first.

### Worked mini-example

Imagine a table of 12 coffee producers with columns: Country, Production (tonnes), Export Share (%), Average Bean Price ($/kg).

**Sub-statement 1:** "More than 5 countries have production above 100,000 tonnes."
Sort by production descending. Count rows ≥ 100,000. If 7 rows, true. If 4 rows, false.

**Sub-statement 2:** "The country with the highest export share also has the highest bean price."
Sort by export share descending. Note the top country. Check its bean price. Now sort by bean price descending. Is the same country at the top? True or false.

**Sub-statement 3:** "At least one country produces more than 200,000 tonnes with a bean price below $3/kg."
Sort by production descending. Look at the top few rows (those above 200,000). Check each one's bean price. If any has a price < $3, true. Otherwise false.

Notice that each sub-statement uses a different sort. Do not try to memorize the whole table — let the sort feature do the work.

Budget: about **2:30 per TA question** including the three sub-statements. If you are spending 4+ minutes, you are probably not using the sort feature.

## Format 4: Graphics Interpretation (GI)

GI shows you a chart — bar, line, scatter, bubble, or some combination — followed by one or two sentences with **fill-in-the-blank dropdowns**. Typically two dropdowns per question, each with 3 to 5 options.

### Strategy: identify the axes before anything else

Every GI question begins with the same move: read the axes. What is on the x-axis? What is on the y-axis? What are the units? What is the scale? Is the y-axis linear or logarithmic? Does it start at zero or at some non-zero baseline?

I cannot emphasize this enough. A huge percentage of GI mistakes come from students who start reading data points before they understand what the axes measure. A bar chart showing "revenue growth (%)" where a 5% bar looks twice as tall as a 3% bar will seduce you into thinking 5 is twice 3 visually, when the test writers deliberately made the baseline non-zero to exaggerate the difference.

### Approximate, don't pinpoint

GI rewards estimation. If a data point looks like it's roughly at 47 on the y-axis, don't agonize over whether it's 46 or 48 — the dropdown options are usually spread far enough apart that approximate is fine. Typical dropdown spreads: "between 30 and 50," "between 50 and 70," "between 70 and 90." Estimation within 5% is usually plenty.

The exception: when dropdown options are clustered tightly ("between 45 and 50," "between 50 and 55"), you need more precision. In that case, take a moment to anchor against gridlines.

### Common chart types you will encounter

- **Bar chart.** Compare heights. Watch for non-zero baselines and stacked vs grouped bars.
- **Line chart.** Track trends over time. Questions often ask about rate of change — steepness of the line between two points.
- **Scatter plot.** Look for correlation direction, strength, and outliers. GI scatter questions often ask, "Is the correlation positive, negative, or approximately zero?"
- **Bubble chart.** Three variables at once — two axes and bubble size. Read all three dimensions before answering.
- **Bar + line combo.** Two y-axes, one on each side of the chart. Bars use one axis, line uses the other. Which y-axis goes with which series? Read the legend carefully.

### Common GI question phrasings

- "The rate of change was greatest between ___ and ___." (Asking about line steepness.)
- "The proportion of ___ that is accounted for by ___ is approximately ___." (Asking about ratios, often between a subset and a total.)
- "If the trend continued, ___ would reach ___ by year ___." (Extrapolation — extend the line past the last data point.)
- "The variable most strongly correlated with ___ is ___." (Scatter or bubble chart reading.)

Budget: about **2:00 per GI question**. They should be the fastest format once you are fluent in chart reading, but students new to GI often over-compute.

## Format 5: Two-Part Analysis (TPA)

TPA presents a scenario — usually a short paragraph — followed by a two-column answer grid with 5 to 7 rows. You pick one row for column 1 and one row for column 2. Both must be correct for credit.

The columns represent two related quantities or roles. Common setups:

- Two values that must satisfy a pair of equations.
- A "cause" and an "effect" in a logical scenario.
- An assumption and a conclusion.
- A minimum and a maximum.
- Two people's ages, speeds, or scores in a word problem.

### Strategy: solve for the relationship first, then scan the grid

The worst TPA approach is to go row by row, checking each option in both columns until you find a pair that works. That is 30 row-checks for a grid with 6 rows, and it eats your clock.

The better approach: set up the relationship between the two columns as an equation or constraint before looking at the grid. Then scan the grid once, filtering for pairs that satisfy the constraint.

### Worked mini-example

> **Scenario:** Alice and Bob contributed money to a joint fund. Alice contributed $X, Bob contributed $Y. The total was $1,200, and Alice contributed three times as much as Bob.
> **Column 1: Alice's contribution.** **Column 2: Bob's contribution.**
> Rows: $100, $200, $300, $400, $800, $900, $1000.

Set up: X + Y = 1,200 and X = 3Y. So 4Y = 1,200, Y = 300, X = 900.

Now scan the rows. For column 1 (Alice), pick $900. For column 2 (Bob), pick $300.

If you tried to do this by trial and error, you would test 7 × 7 = 49 pairs. Equation-first, it is 10 seconds.

### The distractor trap

TPA grids often include values that work for ONE column but not both, or values that satisfy one constraint but not the full relationship. For instance, $400 and $800 sum to $1,200 — so they pass the "total" check. But $800 is not three times $400 ($800 would be twice $400), so they fail the ratio check. A student doing row-by-row trial would spend time on this pair before realizing it fails.

Always verify the final pair against **all** constraints in the scenario, not just the one you used to set up.

### Common TPA question types

- **Quantitative pairs.** Two unknowns, two equations. Solve the system first, then select.
- **Minimum and maximum.** "What is the smallest value for X and the largest for Y consistent with the constraints?" Bound-finding problem.
- **Logical pairs.** "Which statement, if true, would support the conclusion?" and "Which would undermine it?" Read all options and classify each as support, undermine, or neutral.
- **Rate pairs.** Two rates (e.g., speed and time, or price and quantity) that together match a total. Equation-first.

Budget: about **2:30 per TPA question**. TPA tends to be the most time-consuming format in DI, so bank time on DS and GI to afford it.

## The DI Pacing System

Average pacing is 2:15 per question, but average is a trap. Different formats warrant different budgets. Here is the system I use and teach:

- **DS:** aim for 1:45 per question. DS is purely reasoning — no reading overhead. Fast DS gives you time for slower formats.
- **TA:** aim for 2:00 per question, with the sort feature doing most of the work.
- **GI:** aim for 2:00 per question, using estimation rather than exact calculation.
- **MSR:** aim for 2:30 per question — but the total for an MSR set of 3 questions should be ~7 minutes, not 7.5, because the skimming is shared across the set.
- **TPA:** aim for 2:30 per question, with equation-first setup.

These average out slightly under 2:15, which gives you a buffer for the inevitable hard question.

### When to skip

You get up to 3 bookmarks per section, which you can revisit at the end if time allows. Use them strategically:

- **Skip criterion 1:** You have spent 3:00 on a single question and are still not close to an answer. Bookmark, make your best guess, move on.
- **Skip criterion 2:** You read a TA or GI stimulus and realize the data is outside your confidence zone (e.g., a table with unit labels you cannot parse). Bookmark, guess, move on — you can come back.
- **Skip criterion 3:** You encounter an MSR set where the tabs are exceptionally dense and you can tell the 3 questions will eat 10 minutes. If it's one of your first three questions, invest the time. If it's later and you are already behind pace, bookmark all three and guess — then return if you have spare time at the end.

Never spend more than **3:30** on any single question. Even on your "easiest" questions, you should be checking the clock at the 2-minute mark. If you are not on track, accelerate.

### Mid-section checkpoints

At question 7 (roughly one-third through), the clock should read around 29 minutes remaining. At question 14, around 13 minutes remaining. If you are behind these marks, triage ruthlessly for the remainder. If you are ahead, do not slow down — bank the time for the final cluster.

## Your 60-Day DI Practice System

DI rewards structured practice more than any other section, because format familiarity compounds so quickly. Here is the 60-day (roughly 8-week) system I prescribe:

**Weeks 1-2: Drill Data Sufficiency in isolation.**

DS is the single highest-leverage format: it is the most drillable, it appears most frequently, and its five answer choices are eternal. Do 20 DS questions per day, untimed at first, focusing on answer-choice elimination discipline. By the end of week 2, you should be able to cite the five choices verbatim and apply the elimination flow reflexively. Track which answer you choose and which was correct, and look for systematic errors (e.g., "I fall for the C trap 40% of the time").

**Weeks 3-4: One format per day rotation.**

Monday — Table Analysis. Tuesday — Graphics Interpretation. Wednesday — Multi-Source Reasoning. Thursday — Two-Part Analysis. Friday — DS review + hardest missed questions from the week. Weekend — light review, no new material.

For each day, do 10 to 15 questions of the day's format, untimed on Monday-Thursday for the first week, then timed in the second week. Keep a mistake log broken down by format and specific trap.

**Weeks 5-6: Mixed 10-question sets under time.**

Simulate real DI conditions by mixing all five formats in 10-question sets. Strict pacing — 22 minutes per set, which is tighter than real pacing to build speed cushion. After each set, spend twice the set duration reviewing: why you missed what you missed, which format was slowest, which formats you skipped.

**Weeks 7-8: Full-section simulations.**

Two full 20-question DI sections per week, under strict 45-minute timing, on a simulator that mimics the GMAT interface (sortable tables, clickable MSR tabs, on-screen calculator). Between simulations, focus entirely on your mistake log — no new unseen questions.

Three days before test day: stop doing new DI questions. Review your mistake log, re-do 10 warmup questions from formats you are weakest on, and rest.

## Common DI Failure Modes

These are the specific traps I see repeatedly in student performance reviews. Each has a specific fix.

- **Reaching for the calculator when mental math is faster.** Students use the calculator for 2 × 8, which takes 15 seconds of clicking. Use the calculator only for 4+ digit arithmetic or compound percentage chains. For everything else, mental math wins.
- **Misreading a chart's axis — especially non-zero baselines.** A bar chart with a y-axis starting at 80 makes a 90 look "twice as tall" as an 85. Always read the axis range before the bars.
- **Not checking "must be true" vs "could be true" in the question stem.** A sub-statement asking "could exceed 100" needs only one case. A sub-statement asking "must exceed 100" needs every case. Wrong interpretation flips the answer.
- **Treating all DS questions as quantitative.** DS includes yes/no questions ("Is x > 0?") where sufficiency means "definitely yes OR definitely no." Students default to value-finding and miss the yes/no framing.
- **Reading MSR tabs start-to-finish instead of skimming.** Eats 4 minutes before the first question. Skim the structure, then let the questions direct your deep reading.
- **Answering a different question than what was asked.** TPA scenarios often have a third quantity mentioned in the prompt that is not one of the two columns. Students solve for it instead of the column values. Always re-read the column labels before selecting.
- **Not using the TA sort feature.** Trying to verify "more than half" by counting unsorted rows. Wastes 60+ seconds per sub-statement.
- **Picking C on DS without fully checking statements alone.** The C trap — your default suspicion should be that the answer is A, B, or D, and only collapse to C when you have evidence both statements alone fail.

## Practice Recommendation

Head to `/practice` on this platform for DI drills organized by format. Suggested order:

1. **Start with pure DS sets.** These are the fastest to drill and the most format-stable. Aim for 80%+ accuracy on untimed DS before moving on.
2. **Then do single-format sets** for TA, GI, MSR, and TPA — one format at a time, 10 to 15 questions per session, untimed first, then timed.
3. **Then mixed DI sets** that simulate real section distribution.
4. **Finally, full 20-question timed sections** in the last two weeks before your test.

For each question you miss, log the format, the specific trap, and the time you spent. Review the log weekly. Patterns always emerge, and the patterns are your study plan.
