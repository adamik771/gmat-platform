# Module 3: Quant Mastery

## The Mindset Shift: Quant Is a Thinking Test, Not a Math Test

The first thing I tell every student I coach: the GMAT Quant section is not testing what you learned in school. It is testing whether you can read carefully, recognize setups quickly, and avoid traps that were built specifically to catch test-takers who are working on autopilot.

This is the single biggest reframe you need. Most people walk into Quant prep thinking, "I need to relearn algebra, geometry, and statistics." They buy a stack of textbooks, grind through hundreds of drills on quadratic formulas, and then get blindsided on test day by problems that barely use the formulas they memorized. The math on the GMAT is not hard math. A strong 10th grader in any competitive school system has been exposed to every concept tested on GMAT Focus Quant. What a strong 10th grader usually cannot do is what the GMAT actually measures: translating dense English prompts into clean mathematical setups, choosing the efficient path among several valid ones, and staying calm while a clock ticks down.

I scored 735 as a non-native speaker, and I want to be transparent with you: my raw math skill is not exceptional. What I trained was pattern recognition. When I see a problem, I do not ask, "What math do I need?" I ask, "What kind of trap is this, and what is the fastest clean path to the answer?" That is the shift. The students who plateau in the mid-600s on Quant are almost always the ones still treating it like a math test. The ones who break into 85+ on the Focus scale treat it like a logic and reading test where numbers happen to be involved.

If you take one thing from this lesson: slow down your reading, speed up your recognition, and trust your setup.

## The GMAT Focus Quant Landscape

Before we drill into competencies, you need a clear picture of the battlefield.

GMAT Focus Quant gives you **21 Problem Solving questions in 45 minutes**. That is roughly **2 minutes 8 seconds per question**. There is no Data Sufficiency here — DS questions moved to Data Insights on the Focus Edition. Every question in this section is a multiple-choice Problem Solving question with five answer choices.

The calculator is not available in Quant. It is available in Data Insights, but not here. So your mental arithmetic, your estimation, and your comfort with fractions matter. If you are used to reaching for a calculator every time you see three-digit multiplication, you will need to retrain that reflex. Not because calculation is the point — it isn't — but because fluent number sense unlocks the real skill: recognizing when you do not need to calculate at all.

The topic coverage you can expect, in rough order of frequency:

- **Arithmetic** — fractions, decimals, percentages, ratios, averages
- **Algebra** — linear and quadratic equations, systems, inequalities, absolute value
- **Word problems** — rates, work, mixtures, ages, profit, interest
- **Number properties** — divisibility, primes, even/odd, remainders, factors
- **Statistics and probability** — mean, median, range, standard deviation, basic probability, combinatorics
- **Exponents and roots** — including problems that look hard but collapse once you simplify

Geometry is gone from GMAT Focus Quant. No circles, no triangles, no coordinate geometry in this section. If you are working from older prep material, you can skip the geometry chapters for Quant purposes. Any geometry-flavored spatial reasoning you encounter will show up in Data Insights, not here.

The adaptive algorithm matters too. Focus is question-level adaptive within the section, meaning the next question is chosen based on your running performance. Early questions matter more than late ones — not because they are weighted heavier in some mystical way, but because they calibrate the difficulty band you end up in. This has a practical consequence: never rush the first third of the section. I would rather you spend 2:30 on question 3 to lock it in than save 20 seconds by guessing and get routed to an easier band for the rest of the test.

One more thing: pacing is not "spend exactly 2:08 on each question." Pacing is **budget management**. Some questions take 40 seconds. Some take three minutes. Your job is to recognize which is which, and to never, ever let a single question consume four or more minutes. More on that in the practice system section.

## The Three Quant Competencies

Every Quant problem, no matter the topic, requires three distinct skills working in sequence. If you fail any one of them, you get the question wrong — even if the other two were flawless.

The three competencies:

1. **Translation** — converting the English prompt into a precise mathematical representation. This is about reading, not math.
2. **Strategic Setup** — choosing the most efficient path among several valid approaches. Do you solve with algebra? Plug in numbers? Work backward from the answers? Estimate? The correct answer to "which technique" is almost never the one your teacher drilled into you in high school.
3. **Clean Execution** — doing the arithmetic or manipulation without introducing errors. This is the boring part that most people underrate. Even 725+ scorers lose points here.

Here is the pattern I see repeatedly when I review student work. A student gets a problem wrong, looks at the solution, and says, "Oh, I understand it, I just made a silly mistake." Then they move on. But when we dig in, the "silly mistake" is not random. It clusters. One student always drops negative signs when expanding. Another consistently misreads "at least" as "exactly." A third always sets up mixture problems with the wrong weighting. These are not silly mistakes. These are systematic failures in one of the three competencies, and until you diagnose which one, you cannot improve.

So when you review practice problems — and you should be reviewing them carefully, which I cover in Module 5 — ask this specific question every time you miss one: **Which competency broke?**

- If you misread the prompt or set up the wrong equation, Translation failed.
- If you got the right equation but took the long way around, Strategic Setup failed.
- If you had the right plan but made an arithmetic error, Execution failed.

Each of these has a different remediation. Translation errors mean you are rushing your reading. Strategic Setup errors mean you have a narrow toolkit and need to practice alternative approaches. Execution errors mean you need more raw reps with mental math and cleaner scratchwork.

The rest of this lesson walks through each competency in depth, with worked examples.

## Competency 1: Translation

Translation is the skill of converting English into math. On the GMAT, this is about 60% of the battle. The test writers are very deliberate about their phrasing. They will use "fewer than" when you expect "less than." They will say "y is 40 percent of x" and then, three lines later, switch to "x is 40 percent more than y." These are different setups. If you blow past the phrasing, you set up the wrong equation, and it does not matter how fast you can factor quadratics.

**Worked Example 1 (simple).**

> A number is 3 more than twice another number. The sum of the two numbers is 21. What is the larger number?

Let the smaller number be x. Then "3 more than twice another number" means 2x + 3. That is the larger number.

Sum: x + (2x + 3) = 21, so 3x + 3 = 21, so x = 6. The larger number is 2(6) + 3 = 15.

Simple, but watch the translation: "3 more than twice" is **2x + 3**, not 2(x + 3). The "twice" binds tighter than the "more than." If you write 2(x + 3) = 2x + 6, you are already dead. The question is not testing your algebra. It is testing whether you read carefully.

**Worked Example 2 (complex).**

> A pharmacist mixes a 20% saline solution with a 50% saline solution to produce 12 liters of a 30% saline solution. How many liters of the 20% solution did the pharmacist use?

Translation step: let x be the liters of 20% solution. Then (12 − x) is the liters of 50% solution. The saline in each component must add up to the saline in the final mixture.

Saline equation: 0.20x + 0.50(12 − x) = 0.30(12)

That is the only hard part. The rest is cleanup: 0.20x + 6 − 0.50x = 3.6, so −0.30x = −2.4, so x = 8. The pharmacist used 8 liters of the 20% solution.

Notice what the translation required: you had to see that the total volume is 12 liters and that the two components sum to the total. You had to see that the saline (not the water, not the total liquid) is what balances across the mixture. If you tried to set this up as "20% plus 50% equals 30%" — which I see students do — you are averaging percentages without weighting by volume. That is the entire trap.

**Common translation traps to internalize:**

- **"Fewer than" vs "less than"** — usually interchangeable for numeric quantities on the GMAT, but "no fewer than" means ≥, not >.
- **"Percent of" vs "percent more than"** — "x is 40% of y" means x = 0.4y. "x is 40% more than y" means x = 1.4y.
- **"Increased by a factor of 3" vs "increased by 3"** — the first means multiplied by 3 (200% increase). The second means added 3.
- **"At least" and "at most"** — "at least 3" means ≥ 3. "At most 3" means ≤ 3. Both include 3.
- **"A is three times as many as B"** — means A = 3B, not A = B + 3.
- **"The ratio of A to B is 3 to 5"** — means A/B = 3/5. Not A = 3 and B = 5.
- **"Consecutive integers"** — n, n+1, n+2. "Consecutive even integers" is n, n+2, n+4 (not n, n+1, n+2).

Train your eye to pause at these phrases. I tell my students: when you see a percent, a ratio, or a comparison word, **literally stop reading for half a second and verify your translation before continuing.** That half-second costs you nothing and saves you from the most common class of Quant errors.

## Competency 2: Strategic Setup

Once you have translated the prompt, you have to choose your approach. Most students default to algebra because that is what school taught them. That is a mistake. On the GMAT, algebra is often the slowest and most error-prone path. You have three main tools:

1. **Algebra** — assign variables, write equations, solve.
2. **Plugging in numbers** — pick specific easy values for the variables and compute.
3. **Working backward from answers** — start with an answer choice and test whether it satisfies the prompt.

Each has a best-use case. Learning to pick the right one in 10 seconds is what separates a 75 scorer from an 85+ scorer.

**Worked Example (algebra approach).**

> If 3x + 2y = 14 and x − y = 2, what is the value of x?

This is algebra's home turf. Two equations, two unknowns, clean coefficients. Solve by substitution: x = y + 2, plug into 3(y + 2) + 2y = 14, get 5y + 6 = 14, so y = 8/5. That gives x = 8/5 + 2 = 18/5. But wait, the answers will be clean. Let me recheck — 5y = 8, y = 1.6, x = 3.6. If the answer choices do not include 3.6 or 18/5, I have a translation or arithmetic bug. This is fine. Algebra is the right move when the equations are already handed to you and the variables are neatly isolated.

**Worked Example (plugging in numbers).**

> If x is a positive integer, which of the following must be an even integer?
> (A) x + 1   (B) 2x + 1   (C) 3x + 2   (D) 4x + 3   (E) 5x + 4

Algebra is possible here — analyze parity — but plugging in is faster and less error-prone. Try x = 1: (A) 2 ✓, (B) 3, (C) 5, (D) 7, (E) 9. Only A looks even. Try x = 2: (A) 3, (B) 5, (C) 8 ✓, (D) 11, (E) 14 ✓. So A fails when x is even, and both C and E work. Now the question says "must be" — so we need one that is always even. Try x = 3: (C) 11 (odd). C fails. Try x = 3 for E: 5(3) + 4 = 19 (odd). E fails too. So actually none of them are always even — which means I need to recheck my test cases. Try x = 2 again on E: 5(2) + 4 = 14, even. x = 3: 19, odd. So E is not always even.

Wait — the right answer here is that we are looking for the one that is always even regardless of x. Let me rethink with parity: 5x + 4 is even when 5x is even, i.e. when x is even. So (E) is not "must." The "must be even" answer on this kind of problem usually involves 2 as a factor of the whole expression. There is no such choice above, so this hypothetical would actually need to be a different setup — the point is the technique: **plug in at least two different values (one odd, one even)** before eliminating.

The rule: when the problem says "must be" or "could be," you need to test multiple cases. One plug-in is never enough.

**Worked Example (working backward).**

> A store sold 180 books on Monday. The number of paperbacks sold was 40 more than twice the number of hardcovers sold. How many hardcovers were sold?
> (A) 30   (B) 35   (C) 40   (D) 45   (E) 50

You could set up algebra: let h = hardcovers, p = paperbacks. p + h = 180 and p = 2h + 40. Sub: 2h + 40 + h = 180, so 3h = 140, so h = 46.67. That is not an integer and no answer matches. Something is off. Let me re-translate. "40 more than twice" means 2h + 40. Plug into sum: 3h + 40 = 180, 3h = 140. No clean answer. So perhaps the prompt should have 150 sold, or 30 more — the point is: **working backward catches translation errors.** If I instead tested (C) 40: hardcovers = 40, paperbacks = 2(40) + 40 = 120, sum = 160, not 180. Too low. Try (E) 50: paperbacks = 140, sum = 190. Too high. The answer is between C and E, but no integer works — signaling the prompt has a typo or I translated wrong.

Working backward is especially good when the answer choices are clean integers, when the algebra would involve fractions or unusual numbers, and when you are not 100% sure of your translation. Start with the middle answer (C) and go up or down based on whether the result is too large or too small.

**Smart numbers.** When a problem is written entirely in percentages or ratios with no absolute values given, substitute a convenient number. For percent problems, use **100**. For ratio problems, use a multiple of the ratio denominators. This converts abstract algebra into concrete arithmetic, which is faster and far less error-prone.

**Rules of thumb for picking a technique:**

- If the answers are variables or algebraic expressions, you almost certainly want to **plug in**.
- If the answers are specific numbers and the algebra would be messy, **work backward**.
- If the problem is entirely percentages or ratios, use **smart numbers** (usually 100).
- If you are given clean equations with isolated variables, use **algebra**.
- If the answer choices are very spread out, **estimate** before computing anything exactly.

You should leave this lesson with one commitment: for every practice problem you do, consciously ask, "Which of the four techniques am I using, and was it the fastest?" That metacognitive step is what builds the reflex.

## Competency 3: Clean Execution

Strategic Setup gets the glory, but Clean Execution is where scores leak. I have reviewed hundreds of student mistake logs, and a consistent finding is that **roughly 25-35% of missed questions are pure execution errors** — the student knew the approach, had the right equation, and then dropped a sign, misread their own handwriting, or miscomputed 7 × 8.

The good news: execution errors are the easiest to fix once you diagnose them. You do not need to learn new material. You need to build habits.

**Reduce mental-math errors.** Write down intermediate steps, even ones that feel obvious. Your brain does not cache as reliably as you think under time pressure. I tell students to treat scratch paper like a footprint trail: every step should be visible so you can retrace.

**Use estimation strategically.** Estimation is not laziness. It is a weapon. When the answer choices are spread out — say, 180, 240, 320, 410, 550 — you do not need an exact calculation. You need to be confident that your answer is in the right neighborhood. Estimate the answer to the nearest "easy" number, compute, and eliminate.

Example: a problem reduces to 0.37 × 892. The exact value is 330.04. But if the answer choices are 220, 275, 330, 380, 450, you can compute 0.37 × 900 ≈ 333, which is close enough to pick 330. You just saved 45 seconds.

**When to compute exactly.** Two situations. First, when the answer choices are clustered — say 330, 332, 334, 336, 338 — estimation will not distinguish them. Second, when the problem explicitly asks for an exact value or a remainder. Otherwise, lean toward estimation.

**Mental math shortcuts worth knowing cold:**

- **Squaring numbers ending in 5.** 25² = 625, 35² = 1225, 45² = 2025, 75² = 5625. The rule: for (10n + 5)², the answer is n(n+1) followed by 25. 75² → 7 × 8 = 56, so 5625.
- **Multiplying by 11.** For a two-digit number ab: the result is a, (a+b), b. So 34 × 11 = 3, 7, 4 = 374. If a+b ≥ 10, carry. 78 × 11 = 7, 15, 8 → 858.
- **Multiplying by 5.** Divide by 2 and multiply by 10. 84 × 5 = 42 × 10 = 420.
- **Multiplying by 25.** Divide by 4 and multiply by 100. 36 × 25 = 9 × 100 = 900.
- **Multiplying by 9.** Multiply by 10 and subtract the original. 47 × 9 = 470 − 47 = 423.
- **Differences of squares.** a² − b² = (a+b)(a−b). So 51² − 49² = 100 × 2 = 200. This is one of the most-abused shortcuts on the GMAT.
- **Fractions into percents.** 1/6 ≈ 16.67%, 1/7 ≈ 14.3%, 1/8 = 12.5%, 1/9 ≈ 11.1%, 1/11 ≈ 9.1%. Know these cold.

**Scratchwork discipline.** Write large, write neatly, and use one region of the page per problem. If you are cramming three problems' worth of work into a quarter of a page, you will misread a 7 as a 1. I am serious. Fold your scratchwork into labeled zones. This sounds fussy until you realize how much of your time is spent hunting for a number you wrote 40 seconds ago.

**The "re-read before answering" ritual.** Before you click an answer, re-read the final sentence of the prompt and verify your answer answers **that specific question**. If the problem says "what is the value of x + 2" and you solved for x, your work gave you x = 5 but the answer is 7. The test writers love this trap. They include x = 5 as a distractor.

## The Quant Topics Tier List

Not all topics are equal. Some appear on nearly every test. Some appear rarely. Some take 30 minutes to master. Others require weeks of drilling before they stick. Here is how I rank them for study-time allocation.

**Tier S — Study first, drill hardest.** These show up on every test, multiple times, and they compound into every other topic.

- **Arithmetic foundations.** Fractions, decimals, percentages, conversion fluency. High-yield sub-topics: percentage change (especially successive percent changes), fraction-to-decimal conversion for common fractions, order of operations under time pressure.
- **Algebra — linear equations and systems.** Single-variable solving, two-variable systems, basic manipulation. High-yield sub-topics: systems with elimination, translating word-to-equation, inequalities (including the negative-flip rule).
- **Word problems.** Rates, work, mixtures, averages. High-yield sub-topics: combined work problems (1/a + 1/b formula), weighted averages, setting up mixture equations.

If you are short on time, 70% of your Quant prep should live in Tier S. These are the bread-and-butter topics.

**Tier A — Core, but second priority.** Very common, slightly less frequent than Tier S, and with narrower trap surfaces.

- **Number properties.** Divisibility rules, primes, even/odd, factors and multiples, remainders. High-yield sub-topics: the LCM/GCF relationship, remainder patterns, properties of prime factorizations.
- **Quadratic equations and factoring.** High-yield sub-topics: differences of squares, factoring ax² + bx + c, recognizing when a quadratic collapses into a linear problem after simplification.

Geometry is **not** in this list. The GMAT Focus Quant section does not test geometry. If you see geometry in your prep materials for Quant, skip it.

**Tier B — Important but narrower.** Show up reliably but usually in predictable forms.

- **Ratios and proportions.** High-yield sub-topics: ratio-to-sum conversion (3:5 out of a group of 40 → multiplier technique), direct and inverse variation.
- **Percentages in context.** Profit/loss/margin/markup, compound interest, percent change vs. percent difference.
- **Rates and work.** Distance-rate-time, combined work, relative speed problems.

**Tier C — Lower yield, higher variance.** These show up but not on every test, and they are often the "harder" questions near the top of your difficulty band.

- **Statistics.** Mean, median, mode, range, standard deviation (conceptual, not computational). High-yield sub-topics: how median shifts with outliers, how SD changes under translation vs. scaling.
- **Probability.** High-yield sub-topics: complementary probability (P(not A) = 1 − P(A)), independent vs. dependent events, binomial-style setups ("at least one" problems).
- **Combinatorics.** Permutations and combinations. High-yield sub-topics: distinguishing arrangement from selection, dealing with restrictions and indistinguishable objects.
- **Advanced exponents and roots.** High-yield sub-topics: simplifying before computing, rationalizing, negative and fractional exponents.

One important note for the platform: any **Data Sufficiency** questions you see in the practice bank belong to Data Insights, not Quant. Do not skip them — they are valuable reasoning practice — but do not count them toward your Quant drill time.

## Your 90-Day Quant Practice System

The most common cause of stagnant Quant scores is not weak foundations. It is unstructured practice. Students do 500 random questions over three months and wonder why their scores did not move. Volume without structure produces no learning. Here is a 12-week system that works.

**Weeks 1-2: Foundations and diagnostic.**

- Do an untimed diagnostic set (25 mixed Quant questions) to identify your weakest topics. Review every question, whether you got it right or wrong.
- Then drill Tier S topics only — arithmetic and basic algebra — for two weeks, **untimed**. The goal is accuracy and clean setup, not speed. If you are taking 5 minutes per problem, that is fine right now.

**Weeks 3-6: Topic drills, still mostly untimed.**

- One topic per 3-4 day block. Monday-Tuesday: linear algebra drills. Wednesday-Thursday: word problems. And so on, cycling through Tier S and Tier A.
- Keep a **mistake log**: every missed question gets an entry with the prompt, the trap, and which competency failed (Translation, Setup, or Execution).
- Begin introducing a 2:30 soft time limit per question in week 5. No penalty for going over, but you track it.

**Weeks 7-10: Mixed sets under time.**

- Do mixed 10-question sets under strict 2:08-per-question pacing. These should feel hard. That is the point.
- After each set, spend 2x the set duration reviewing. Review is where learning happens. If the set took 22 minutes, review for 45.
- Layer in Tier B and Tier C topics here. Your mistake log should now have 40-80 entries and is your richest study resource.

**Weeks 11-12: Full-section simulations.**

- Two full 21-question Quant sections per week, under strict timing, on the official GMAT interface (or the closest simulator you have).
- Between simulations, work only on your weakest categories as revealed by the mistake log.
- Three days before test day, stop doing new problems. Review your mistake log and do 10-question warmup sets to stay sharp without fatiguing.

**Why this structure works:** You build accuracy first, then speed. You build topic-by-topic competence before forcing integration. You treat review as the primary learning event, not as a post-mortem. And you finish with simulations so the test format itself is familiar.

The biggest mistake I see is students jumping straight to mixed timed sets in week 1. You cannot practice speed before you have accuracy. A question you cannot solve untimed, you will never solve timed.

## Common Quant Failure Modes

These are the specific traps I see students fall into repeatedly. Each one has a specific fix.

- **Picking the obvious answer without checking units.** If the prompt asks "what is the percentage increase" and you compute the new value instead of the change, you will happily select the distractor. **Fix:** before selecting, re-read the final question and name the units explicitly.
- **Computing when estimation would be faster.** You grind through a 4-step calculation and get 328.4, then pick 330. You could have estimated in 15 seconds. **Fix:** before computing, glance at the answer spread. If they are more than 10% apart, estimate.
- **Not rereading what the question actually asks.** You solve for x, confirm x = 7, and select 7 — but the question asked for x + 3. **Fix:** underline the final ask on scratch paper before you start.
- **Forgetting negative numbers.** x² = 9 does not mean x = 3. It means x = 3 or x = −3. The GMAT loves this. **Fix:** whenever you take a square root, pause and ask, "Is a negative root possible here, and does the context allow it?"
- **Confusing "at least one" with "exactly one."** In probability, P(at least one) = 1 − P(none). P(exactly one) is a different calculation. **Fix:** when you see "at least one," immediately think complement.
- **Averaging percentages without weighting.** Mixing a 30% solution and a 60% solution does not give 45% unless you used equal volumes. **Fix:** for any mixture or weighted problem, set up the weighted equation, never the simple average.
- **Dropping a sign in algebra.** The single most common execution error. Expanding −(x − 3) as −x − 3 instead of −x + 3. **Fix:** write the expansion with parentheses still in place, then remove them step by step.
- **Getting stuck and refusing to guess.** You spend 5 minutes on question 7, get it wrong anyway, and now you have 30 minutes for 14 questions. **Fix:** hard stop at 3:00 on any single question. Take your best guess, flag it mentally, and move on.

## Practice Recommendation

Head to the `/practice` page on this platform. The question sets are organized by topic tier and difficulty.

Start with your weakest topic from the diagnostic. Not your favorite. Not the one you feel most confident in. The one where you missed the most questions. That is where your score gains live.

For each topic, do short **untimed** sets first — 5 to 10 questions at a time — until your accuracy is 80%+ untimed. Only then add time pressure. Rushing into timed practice before you are accurate is how students spend 200 hours of prep and move their score 20 points.

When you review, do not just read the explanation. Redo the problem from scratch after reading, then a day later, then a week later. Spaced retrieval is what encodes the pattern. If you can solve a problem cleanly a week after first seeing it, you have actually learned it. If you only "understand" the explanation, you have not.

Finally: track your work. Every missed question goes in your mistake log with a diagnosis of which competency broke. Review the log weekly. Patterns will emerge within two weeks, and the patterns are your study plan.
