# Module 6: Mock Exam Strategy

## Why Mocks Are More Valuable Than Any Single Study Session

Let me start with the claim that drives this entire module: a single 2-hour-15-minute mock, taken under real conditions and analyzed properly, will teach you more about your weaknesses than 20 hours of topical drilling. That is not hyperbole. I have watched students grind through hundreds of practice questions, all of them scored correctly on drill sets, and then bomb a full-length simulation because the test is not a sum of its parts. The test is a compound event, and only a mock surfaces what compounding does to your performance.

Here is what topical drilling cannot tell you:

- **Pacing under cumulative pressure.** A question that takes you 2:30 when it is the only one on your screen is a very different question at minute 70, when you are already 4 minutes behind budget and have two more sections to go. The arithmetic is the same. Your capacity to execute it has changed.
- **Mental stamina.** Your brain glucose starts to dip somewhere around minute 45 of the first section if you did not fuel correctly. By section 3, you are often thinking with a noticeably dimmer instrument than you had at the start. Drills never reveal this, because drills end long before the fatigue arrives.
- **Section-switching fatigue.** Coming out of Quant and into Verbal requires a mode flip — from numeric recognition to argument structure parsing. In isolation, both are easy. Back-to-back, with only a 10-minute break, they collide. The first three questions of your second section are often the most expensive questions on the entire test because your brain has not switched modes yet.
- **Decision-making under uncertainty.** On a drill, you know you have 20 questions in front of you. On a mock, you do not know how the adaptive algorithm is routing you, you cannot see what is coming next, and every decision — guess or fight, skip or grind — has downstream consequences. Drills do not teach this. Only a full simulation does.

So here is the mental reframe I want you to walk into this module with: mocks are not score predictors. They are the single highest-signal learning event in your prep. A student who takes six mocks and analyzes each of them deeply will almost always outperform a student who takes twelve mocks and just looks at the score at the end. The analysis is three times more valuable than the mock itself, which is why we will spend the bulk of this module on the analysis protocol rather than the act of taking the test.

The students who gain 50 to 100 points in their final six weeks are not the students who studied the most. They are the students who used mocks as diagnostic instruments — as a mirror held up to their actual test-day behavior — and then methodically drilled the specific weaknesses that mirror revealed. That is the meta-skill of this module.

## The Mock Taxonomy: Official vs Third-Party

Not all mocks are created equal, and choosing the wrong mock at the wrong time will corrupt your data. You need to understand the three tiers.

**Official GMAT Focus practice exams (mba.com).** There are six of these available, sold as two sets of three. These are the real test. Same interface, same adaptive algorithm, same item pool flavor, same scoring model. The screen you see on your home laptop when you fire up an official practice exam is, to the pixel, the screen you will see at the test center or on your remote-proctored attempt. When mba.com reports a score, that score is as close to a real predictor as exists on the market. Treat these six mocks like a finite, precious resource, because they are.

My rule: **save at least four of your six official mocks for the final six weeks of prep.** The score from an official mock taken in week 12 of a 12-week plan is vastly more informative than one taken in week 3, because by week 3 you are still in heavy content review and your score is not yet reflective of your eventual ability. Burning an official mock early is like using your best wine to cook a stew — it works, but you have wasted something that cannot be replenished.

**Third-party mocks (Manhattan Prep, Target Test Prep, Kaplan, Veritas, Princeton Review).** These are good supplements. The questions are usually high quality — Manhattan Prep and TTP in particular produce items that rival official questions for diagnostic value — but **their scoring algorithms run significantly harder than the official test.** It is extremely common for a student who is sitting on a genuine 685 in official mocks to post a 545 on a Manhattan Prep exam and panic. The panic is misplaced. The Manhattan score is not wrong, exactly — it is calibrated to a different curve. A 150 to 200 point gap below your official score is within normal range for third-party products.

The right way to use third-party mocks: **use their questions for pattern recognition and pacing practice, but do not use their scores for prediction.** Specifically, I want you to:

- Take third-party mocks in the early and middle phases of prep, when you need volume and exposure to diverse question styles, but your score prediction does not yet matter.
- Review the questions with the same rigor as you would an official mock. The educational value is real.
- Ignore the three-digit score. Look at per-section timing, per-question-type accuracy, and your error clusters. Those data points transfer. The composite score does not.

**In-app and adaptive tools (including the practice page on this platform).** These are drill sets, not mocks. They are phenomenal for targeted skill-building between mocks — narrow, repeatable, and adjustable by topic and difficulty — but a 20-question topic drill is not a mock no matter how adaptive it is. Do not count it in your mock budget. Count it as a drill.

The taxonomy in one sentence: **official mocks for calibration, third-party mocks for volume, in-app drills for skill-building. Never confuse which role you are asking a tool to play.**

## The 6-Mock Calendar

Here is the specific schedule I recommend for a 12-week prep. If your prep is shorter, compress; if it is longer, stretch the early mocks but keep the final three anchored to the last three weeks.

**Mock 1 — Week -12 (or earliest): baseline diagnostic.** This is the one covered in Module 2. Take this before you have done any real studying. Your goal is not to score well. Your goal is to establish a before-picture so that future mocks have something to compare against. A reasonable choice here is a third-party mock or an older official free mock (the two free ones that come with the mba.com account) — you want to preserve your paid official mocks for later. Expect to be uncomfortable. That is the point.

**Mock 2 — Week -8: first checkpoint after initial topic drilling.** By this point you should have completed your first full pass through Quant, Verbal, and Data Insights content. The purpose of Mock 2 is to check whether the content review is starting to translate into test-taking performance. Expect improvement from your baseline, but do not be surprised if the improvement is smaller than you hoped — many students pick up 20 to 40 points here, not 80. That is normal. This should still be a third-party mock or a free official mock.

**Mock 3 — Week -6: expect a plateau; this is normal.** This is the mock that derails the most students emotionally. You have now been studying hard for six weeks, you took Mock 2 and saw modest improvement, and you expect Mock 3 to continue the trend. Often it does not. Often Mock 3 sits within 20 points of Mock 2 or even slightly below. **This is not failure.** It is the normal learning curve. Skill acquisition is not linear — it plateaus while underlying patterns consolidate, and then it jumps. If you know to expect the plateau, you will not panic. If you do not, you will often derail your own prep by abandoning a plan that was actually working.

**Mock 4 — Week -4: testing refined timing and strategy.** Start using official mocks here. The purpose of Mock 4 is to stress-test the pacing adjustments and strategic choices you have made in response to Mocks 2 and 3. If Mock 3 showed you were losing 4 minutes on Verbal RC, Mock 4 is where you test whether your new RC approach recovered those minutes. Think of Mock 4 as a controlled experiment.

**Mock 5 — Week -2: final dress rehearsal with full test-day conditions.** Official mock. This is the single most important mock of your prep. You take it exactly as you will take the real test: same start time, same breakfast, same clothing, same break protocol, same environment. The score from Mock 5 is your most reliable test-day prediction, so treat it accordingly — neither celebrate an outlier high nor spiral on an outlier low.

**Mock 6 — Week -1 (optional): a "fresh" run 5 days before the real test.** Whether to take Mock 6 depends on you. Some students benefit from one more full-length rep to stay sharp. Others find that a sixth mock too close to game day produces fatigue that lingers into the test. My default advice: **take Mock 6 only if you have been recovering well from mocks and can do it 5 full days before the test, with a hard taper afterward.** If you take it, take it for the timing practice and the nerves rehearsal — do not expect to learn anything new content-wise. Do not take a mock within 72 hours of your test. Ever. The fatigue risk outweighs any upside.

A quick heuristic for which mock to take when: **official mocks go in the final four slots (Mocks 3 through 6 if you have the budget, or 4 through 6 at minimum). Third-party mocks fill the first two or three slots.** Preserve the six official mocks like ammunition — you only get six, and the ones you take last are the ones that matter.

## Test-Day Simulation Protocol

The difference between a mock that predicts your real score and a mock that misleads you is almost entirely about simulation fidelity. A mock taken in sweatpants on your bed at 2 p.m. with your cat walking across the keyboard is not the same event as a mock taken at your desk at 9 a.m. with the door closed. Both may produce a score. Only one of them tells you anything real.

Here is the protocol, and I want you to follow it tightly from Mock 3 onward:

- **Match your real start time.** If you are taking the real test at 10 a.m., take your mocks at 10 a.m. This is non-negotiable for Mocks 4, 5, and 6. Your cognition has a circadian profile — you are sharper at some hours than others — and you want to calibrate your practice to the hour you will actually perform. A 685 at 11 a.m. is not the same person as a 685 at 8 p.m.
- **Eat what you will eat on test day, and test it in advance.** Settle your breakfast well before Mock 5. I once coached a student who decided to try a new "performance" breakfast shake on the morning of his real test — three hours in, his stomach was in revolt and he lost an estimated 30 points to discomfort. Test the breakfast. Test the coffee. Test the snack you will eat on the break. Do not introduce variables on game day.
- **Wear what you will wear.** Sounds silly. Matters anyway. If you plan to wear a hoodie because test centers can be cold, wear the hoodie on Mock 5. Your body needs to have already run the test in these exact clothes.
- **Use only the official 10-minute breaks.** The real test gives you two optional 10-minute breaks between sections. You can take zero, one, or two. Whichever you plan to take on test day, take that same pattern in your mocks. Do not pause for a snack in the middle of Verbal. Do not grab your phone between questions 12 and 13.
- **No pausing, no redoes, no skipping forward and coming back.** The mock is a continuous event. If you pause the timer to look something up, you are not taking a mock — you are doing open-book practice. Both have value; do not confuse them.
- **Environment: quiet room, door closed, phone off, not your usual study spot.** This last one is subtle and important. If you study at the same desk every day, that desk has cognitive associations with learning and review — it is a low-stakes environment. A real test is a high-stakes environment. I recommend taking at least Mocks 4, 5, and 6 at a **different** desk — a library, a co-working space, a friend's kitchen table — somewhere that has "test" energy, not "study" energy. It sounds woo. It is not.

The principle underneath all of this: **test day is a performance, and mocks are dress rehearsals.** A dress rehearsal in pajamas is not a dress rehearsal. The whole point is to remove novelty from test day so that on test day, the only novel thing is the adrenaline. Everything else — the time, the food, the chair, the clothes, the rhythm — should feel like the fourth or fifth time you have done this, because it is.

## The 3-Day Mock Analysis Protocol

This is the section most students skip, and skipping it is the single biggest waste of effort in GMAT prep. The analysis is three times more valuable than the mock itself. If you take a mock and do not analyze it, you have paid the full cost — the time, the fatigue, the stress — and collected roughly a third of the value. A disciplined three-day protocol is what converts a mock from a 2-hour event into a week-long learning engine.

**Day of mock — same day, 10 minutes of reflection only.** Log the score, by section and composite. Then write three things, unprompted and fast: how your energy tracked through the test (when did you feel fresh, when did you feel gassed), how your confidence tracked (which questions felt shaky even when you got them right), and how your pacing felt (where were you ahead, where were you behind). Ten minutes. That is the whole entry. **Do not review questions.** You are emotionally charged, tired, and your pattern recognition is degraded. Any "analysis" you do today will be contaminated by recency bias — you will obsess over the questions you remember instead of the questions that actually matter. Close the laptop and go for a walk.

**Day +1 — full question-by-question review (2 to 3 hours).** This is the engine. Go through every question on the test, one at a time.

- **For every wrong question**, categorize the error into exactly one of five buckets: **conceptual** (I did not know the content), **careless** (I knew it, I slipped), **time-pressure** (I rushed because I was behind), **misread** (I translated the prompt wrong), or **strategy** (I picked the wrong approach and burned time). Write the category next to the question number in your log. You will need this pattern data tomorrow.
- **For every right question**, flag the ones you got right by luck or shaky reasoning. These are "false positives" — questions that happened to land correctly but where your process was weak. False positives are the most dangerous data points in your entire prep because they inflate your score without corresponding skill. On the real test, under real pressure, false positives flip to wrongs.
- For every question you flag (wrong or false positive), **re-solve it from scratch without looking at the explanation first.** If you can now solve it cleanly, the issue was execution or pacing. If you still cannot solve it, the issue is conceptual — flag it for drill.
- Then, and only then, read the official explanation. Sometimes the official path is faster than yours. Learn it even if you got the question right.

**Day +2 — pattern synthesis (60 to 90 minutes).** Now you zoom out. Lay your categorized wrong-question list and false-positive list next to each other. Ask four questions:

- **Which sections dropped in timing?** Did Verbal run long? Did Quant finish with four minutes to spare, which means you were over-paced and probably rushing? Section-level pacing patterns are where most recoverable time lives.
- **Which question types had the most errors?** Did 4 of your 6 Verbal wrongs come from Critical Reasoning Assumption questions? Did 3 of your 5 Quant wrongs come from weighted averages? These clusters are your drill plan.
- **What was the "I should have gotten that" pattern?** These are the wrongs that cluster in the "careless" and "misread" buckets. Usually they have a theme — dropping a negative sign, confusing "at least" with "exactly," missing a "not" in a CR stimulus. Name the theme.
- **Where did the false positives cluster?** If three of your false positives were Data Sufficiency, you have a DS fluency problem even though your DS score looks fine. That is the hidden leak Mock 5 will punish if you ignore it.

**Day +3 — create the drill plan for the next week.** Convert the Day +2 patterns into specific drill sets. Not "do more Verbal" — that is not a plan. The plan looks like: "15 Critical Reasoning Assumption questions from the platform's Tier-A bank, untimed, with full written justification for each answer" or "20 weighted-average word problems, timed at 2:15 each, with error log entries for every miss." Specific. Drillable. Measurable.

Here is a worked example from a real student pattern I see constantly. Call her Priya — she scored a 645 on Mock 3 and was crushed because Mock 2 was 655. Day-of reflection: "Felt fine on Quant, energy crashed at start of Verbal." Day +1 review categorized her 11 Verbal misses as: 2 conceptual (RC inference), 4 careless (3 misread "except" questions in CR, 1 dropped a "not"), 3 time-pressure (rushed the last 4 RC questions), 2 strategy (tried to eliminate bottom-up when top-down would have been faster). Day +2 synthesis: the real problem was not content — it was that she was losing 4 minutes on the first RC passage because she was over-reading, then panicking for the rest of the section. The "conceptual" 2 were actually time-pressure misses she had mislabeled. Day +3 plan: 6 days of RC speed-drill with a 3-minute cap on passage reading, plus a CR negation-phrase drill set to kill the "except/not" misreads. Mock 4, two weeks later: 690. The score did not jump because she got smarter. It jumped because she fixed a timing leak the mock diagnosed.

**Mock-without-analysis is wasted practice.** Burn this into your calendar: every mock is a 4-day event, not a 1-day event.

## How to Read Your Mock Data

Your mock report gives you more than a score. It gives you telemetry. Here is what to look at, in priority order.

**Section scores plus timing, analyzed as the four quadrants from Module 2.** Cross-reference accuracy and pace: questions answered quickly and correctly (your strength zone), quickly and incorrectly (rushing errors — high-value fix), slowly and correctly (time drains that you got away with, but only barely), and slowly and incorrectly (the worst quadrant — both slow and wrong, usually a sign of a concept gap). At the section level, ask: was Quant in your fast-right zone or slow-wrong zone? The quadrant distribution of your wrong answers tells you what kind of intervention you need. Rushing errors want slowdown drills. Slow-wrong errors want content drills.

**Question-type accuracy.** Any decent mock breaks out performance by question type — CR Assumption vs Weaken vs Inference, RC Detail vs Main Idea vs Inference, Quant by subtopic, Data Insights by format (Multi-Source, Table Analysis, Graphics, Two-Part, Data Sufficiency). Build a simple spreadsheet over your last three mocks. If you are hitting 80% on CR Strengthen but 40% on CR Assumption, your drill plan writes itself. Do not average accuracy across a section and call it a day — the average hides the real data.

**The "cluster" heuristic.** If you got 3 or more questions wrong in a row — what I call a "cluster" — you were not in a content gap. You were in a timing or cognitive-fatigue spiral. Clusters are almost always caused by one question that rattled you, usually a 3-minute grinder you refused to guess on. The 3 subsequent wrongs are collateral damage. The fix for a cluster is not "study the three topics those three questions covered." The fix is "guess and move on at 2:45 next time." Clusters are a symptom; the disease is one upstream decision.

**The endurance curve.** For each section, plot accuracy by question position — the first third, middle third, and last third. If your last third drops by more than 15 percentage points from your first third, you have a stamina problem, not a content problem. This is extremely common on Verbal, which is usually the second or third section and lands at the 90-to-135-minute mark. Stamina problems are fixed by doing back-to-back drill blocks in practice — not by studying more content. If you fix your first two sections' pacing so you arrive at your last section with more glucose and composure, the last-third accuracy will recover without you learning anything new.

**The false-positive count.** From your Day +1 review, count how many questions you got right through guessing or shaky reasoning. Track this across mocks. If your false-positive count is 5, your "real" accuracy is 5 points lower than your scored accuracy. If it is 10, you are running on borrowed time and the real test will call in the loan. Driving your false-positive count down is often the single fastest way to convert a brittle 695 into a durable 705.

## Strategic Pacing During a Mock

Pacing is not "spend exactly 2:08 per question." Pacing is budget management across a section. Here are the numbers you should know cold.

**Section-level pace budgets for GMAT Focus:**

- Quant: 45 minutes for 21 questions. That is roughly **2:08 per question** as a soft average. I like to check in at question 7 (should be around 15 minutes in), question 14 (30 minutes in), and question 21 (under 45). If you are more than 90 seconds behind your checkpoint, you adjust. Not at question 20. Right now.
- Verbal: 45 minutes for 23 questions. That is roughly **1:57 per question**. Same checkpoint logic: question 8 (around 16 minutes), question 15 (30 minutes), finish at 45.
- Data Insights: 45 minutes for 20 questions. That is roughly **2:15 per question**, which feels generous but deceives you — DI questions are often multi-part and can eat 3:30 if you let them. Check in at question 7 (around 16 minutes) and question 14 (32 minutes).

**The "rush last" rule.** If you are behind at question 10, adjust now, not at question 20. The most common pacing failure mode is to notice you are 3 minutes behind at question 18 and then try to make up 3 minutes in the last 3 questions. That does not work — you rush all three, get two of them wrong, and finish on time with worse accuracy than if you had just guessed on one and moved on. Rule: **adjustments made before the midpoint are free; adjustments made in the final third are expensive.** Make them early.

**When to skip and come back.** Specific criteria: skip when you have spent 1:30 and have not made visible progress, when the problem type is one of your known weaknesses and the alternative is burning 2 more minutes for a low-probability correct answer, or when you notice yourself re-reading the stem for the third time. **Do not skip because a problem looks hard on first glance.** Some of the most answerable problems look intimidating for 10 seconds and then collapse. The skip decision is based on elapsed time, not aesthetics.

**Reading the clock, with discipline.** Glance at the clock every 4 to 5 questions, not every question. Checking too often creates anxiety and breaks flow. Checking too rarely lets drift accumulate. The rhythm is: solve, solve, solve, solve, glance-and-reset. Your brain should know roughly where it is without verifying, and the glance is a calibration, not a surprise.

**Panic management: the "deep breath plus reread the stem" protocol.** The moment you notice panic — racing heart, re-reading without absorbing, blurred focus — do exactly this: close your eyes for 2 seconds, take one deep breath that visibly moves your shoulders, then re-read the stem one sentence at a time. Panic costs you maybe 15 seconds per episode. Panic plus failing to arrest it costs you the rest of the section. The breath is cheap. Take it.

## Common Mock Failure Modes

Specific traps, each with a specific fix.

- **Taking a mock after a long study session.** Fatigue confound. Your score is 20 to 40 points below your real ability, and now you are demoralized for no good reason. Fix: mocks are standalone events. No heavy study in the 24 hours before a mock.
- **Taking mocks only on weekends.** If your real test is on a Tuesday morning, you have never practiced Tuesday-morning performance. Fix: at least two of your final three mocks should be on the day of the week and at the exact hour of your real test.
- **Obsessing over the composite score instead of analyzing the pattern.** The score is a single number. Your performance has dozens of informative dimensions. Staring at "655" tells you nothing you can act on. Fix: ban yourself from mentioning the score until Day +2 of the analysis protocol. Talk about sections and patterns, not composites.
- **Taking 5 mocks in 2 weeks.** Diminishing returns at best, overtraining at worst. You cannot learn faster than your analysis cadence supports, and the analysis takes 3 days per mock. Fix: cap at two mocks per week during peak prep, one per week in the final fortnight.
- **Not simulating test conditions.** Covered above. Casual mocks produce casual data. Fix: the protocol earlier in this module is not a suggestion.
- **Not reviewing right answers.** You miss all your false positives. Fix: Day +1 review includes every question, not just wrongs.
- **Retaking the same mock too soon.** The seen-questions bias inflates your score by 40 to 80 points and destroys the diagnostic value. Fix: never retake a mock within 60 days, and prefer to never retake an official mock at all. Six official mocks, used once each, is enough.
- **Giving up after a bad mock instead of analyzing it.** The bad mock is the most valuable mock. It exposes more than a good one does. Fix: when the score surprises you downward, treat it as a gift. The analysis from a bad mock usually unlocks the next 30 points.

## The Plateau-Breaking Playbook

Suppose you have taken two mocks in a row and the score has not moved. Mock 3 was 665, Mock 4 was 670. You are doing the work. The number is stuck. What now?

**Likely causes, in order of frequency.** First, **insufficient topic drill** — you are doing mixed practice when you should be doing focused weak-area drilling. If your mistake log shows 40% of wrongs coming from three specific question types and you are not drilling those question types daily, the mock cannot improve. Second, **stale mental approach** — you have plateaued on a strategy that worked up to this level and will not work past it. Common example: students who guess too conservatively on hard Quant questions and let the clock run down on easier ones they could have secured. Third, **a pacing issue masquerading as a content gap** — you think your Verbal is weak on inference questions, but actually you are rushing the last four inference questions in every section because of a 90-second deficit accumulated from over-reading the first passage. Fourth, **overtrained on comfort areas** — you have drilled the topics you already know 85% accuracy on, because they feel rewarding, while avoiding the topics you know 50% accuracy on, because they feel bad. Comfort-area drilling adds maybe 3 points. Weak-area drilling adds 20 to 40.

**Diagnosis protocol.** Put Mock 3 and Mock 4 side by side on a single spreadsheet — row per question type, column per mock, cells with accuracy and average time. Look for patterns that repeat across both mocks, not patterns that appear in one and not the other. A repeated pattern is the diagnosis. A one-time pattern is noise. If both mocks show you losing minutes on Data Sufficiency and missing more than half of your CR Assumption questions, you have named the root cause. Write it down as a single sentence: "I am losing 3 minutes per section to DS over-grinding, and my CR Assumption accuracy is 40% because I confuse necessary with sufficient."

**Intervention options.** Based on the diagnosis, pick one of four:

- **A specific drill plan.** Seven to 10 days of focused drilling on the identified weakness. 30 to 40 questions per topic, reviewed with the Day +1 protocol. No mocks during this window. You are not trying to predict your score — you are trying to repair a specific subskill.
- **Switch study modality.** If you have been doing mostly reading-based review (lessons, explanations), switch to active problem-solving. If you have been doing mostly problem-solving, switch to written explanation — force yourself to explain aloud, in full sentences, why the right answer is right and the wrongs are wrong. Modality switches break stale patterns.
- **Take 3 days off.** Zero prep. This sounds like giving up. It is not. Plateau is often cognitive fatigue dressed up as a content gap. Three days of true rest — no questions, no lessons, no podcasts — can reset your ceiling more than three days of forced drilling ever will. I have seen students jump 30 points in the mock after a deliberate break. Not because they magically got smarter. Because they stopped leaking the 30 points they were losing to chronic fatigue.
- **Work with a coach or a tutor for a diagnostic session.** An outside pair of eyes will see patterns you cannot. One hour with someone who watches you solve ten problems live can identify a process flaw that has been invisible to you for months.

**My own plateau story.** I had my own plateau at roughly 680. Two mocks in a row, same score, and I was deeply frustrated. I had been drilling hard — probably 20 hours a week on top of a full-time job. What I finally diagnosed, after a painful side-by-side review with a coach, was that I was over-verifying my CR answers. I would pick the right answer in 80 seconds and then spend another 40 seconds "just checking" before committing. Across a section that is nearly 4 lost minutes, which became 2 rushed questions at the end, which became 2 wrong. The fix was not content. The fix was a rule: "commit at 90 seconds." Within two mocks I was at 705, and within another three I was testing in the 720s. From 680 to 730 was not a straight line, and the jump did not come from learning new math. It came from a single behavioral change that a mock-plus-analysis loop surfaced. That is the whole argument for this module in one anecdote.

## This Week's Homework

Four concrete actions, this week:

- **Schedule your next mock.** Put it on the calendar right now, with a specific start time that matches (or approximates) your real test time. If you do not know your real test time yet, pick 9 a.m. as your default. Block the full 2 hours and 30 minutes, plus 30 minutes on either side for setup and cool-down.
- **Block the 3-day analysis window.** The mock itself is 3 hours on Day 0. Then block: 10 minutes on Day 0 evening for the reflection entry, 2 to 3 hours on Day +1 for question-by-question review, 60 to 90 minutes on Day +2 for pattern synthesis, and 60 minutes on Day +3 for drill-plan creation. If your schedule cannot accommodate those blocks, **reschedule the mock** until it can. Taking the mock without the analysis is not just suboptimal — it is a waste of the mock.
- **Preview the analysis protocol before you take the mock.** Open this module to the 3-Day Analysis section the morning of the mock, so the categories (conceptual / careless / time-pressure / misread / strategy) are fresh. Reviewing with the categories already loaded is twice as fast as reviewing without them.
- **Pick the right mock for your stage.** If this is Mock 1 or Mock 2, take a third-party or free official mock. If this is Mock 4, 5, or 6, take a paid official mock. Do not waste a paid official on an early-stage run where your score is not yet reflective of your trained ability.

One more thing. If you have been taking mocks without analyzing them, restart the protocol from the next mock. Do not try to retroactively analyze old mocks — the data is too stale and your memory of the experience is already distorted. Start fresh, do it right, and the next six weeks of prep will produce more score movement than the previous twelve did.
