# Module 6: Mock Exam Strategy

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the purpose of mock exams (and why most people misuse them)
- Build a proper mock cadence that peaks on test day
- Master the debrief process that extracts maximum learning from every mock
- Avoid the top 5 mistakes test-takers make with practice tests

---

## The Real Purpose of a Mock

Most people think mocks exist to predict their score. That's wrong. A mock is a **diagnostic tool**. Its primary job is to expose:
- Which topics you're still weak on
- Where your pacing breaks down
- How your performance holds up under pressure over 2 hours and 15 minutes
- What question types you should prioritize in the next study block

The score is a byproduct. If you take a mock and only look at the number, you're wasting 80% of its value.

---

## How Many Mocks to Take (and When)

### The Cadence

**Ideal cadence for a 3-month prep:**
- **Week 1:** Diagnostic (baseline)
- **Week 4-5:** Mock 1 (check progress after initial content review)
- **Week 7-8:** Mock 2 (mid-prep checkpoint)
- **Week 10:** Mock 3 (simulate test conditions)
- **Week 11:** Mock 4 (final checkpoint)
- **Week 12:** Test day

That's 4-5 mocks total, plus your diagnostic. More than that is usually counterproductive.

### Why Not More?
1. **Mock burnout.** Each full mock is 2 hours 15 minutes plus 1-2 hours of debrief. Take too many and you exhaust yourself.
2. **Diminishing returns.** If you're not learning from each mock, doing another one won't help.
3. **Score drift.** Taking a mock while tired gives you a misleadingly low score, which hurts your confidence.

### Which Mocks to Use
- **Official GMAT Focus practice tests (mba.com):** These are the closest to the real thing. Take these in the final 3-4 weeks when your score estimates matter most.
- **Third-party mocks (Manhattan, Kaplan, Veritas, etc.):** Use these earlier for volume. Their scoring algorithms aren't identical to the real test, but the questions are good practice.

**Rule:** Save your official mocks for later in prep. Don't "waste" a good official mock early when you're still in heavy content review mode.

## Test Conditions Matter

A mock taken in your bedroom with your dog on your lap at 2pm is not the same as a mock taken at 8am in a quiet room with a timer running and no breaks beyond what's allowed.

### Simulating Real Conditions

**Environment:**
- Quiet room — no phone, no notifications, no interruptions
- Chair and desk setup similar to a test center (no couch)
- Minimal distractions (warn housemates in advance)

**Timing:**
- Start at the same time you'd start on test day (usually morning)
- Take only the breaks allowed by the real test (one 10-minute break after the first section)
- Don't pause the timer mid-question to look something up — that's cheating yourself

**Mental state:**
- Don't take a mock when you're exhausted or distracted
- Don't take a mock "cold" right after waking up — warm up with a few practice questions or a light review
- Bring water and a snack, just like test day

If you can't simulate these conditions, acknowledge it when reading your score. A mock taken casually is still valuable for learning, but the score is less reliable.

## The Debrief: Where the Real Learning Happens

Taking the mock is only half the work. The debrief — analyzing your performance afterward — is where you actually improve.

### The 3-Hour Debrief

Most people spend 15 minutes "reviewing" a mock. That's not enough. A proper debrief takes 2-3 hours. Here's the process:

### Step 1: Cool Down (30 minutes to a few hours)
Don't start the debrief immediately after finishing. Your emotions are too raw. Take a break, eat something, do anything else.

### Step 2: Look at Section Scores First (5 minutes)
How did each section go? Were there any surprises? Which section was weakest? This gives you the macro picture.

### Step 3: Review Every Wrong Question (90+ minutes)
For each question you got wrong, do the following:
1. **Re-read the question without looking at the answer choices.** Can you solve it correctly now?
2. **If yes:** This was an execution error. Note it in your error log under the relevant category (careless, misread, time pressure, etc.).
3. **If no:** This is a content gap. Go back to the relevant lesson or guide. Study the concept. Then come back and redo the question.
4. **For every question (right or wrong):** Read the official explanation. Sometimes the "official" approach is faster than the one you used. Learn it.

### Step 4: Review Questions You Got Right But Weren't Sure About (30 minutes)
These are "lucky" questions. You got them right, but could easily get them wrong next time. Go back and understand why the correct answer is correct. Don't let luck fool you.

### Step 5: Analyze Timing Patterns (15 minutes)
Most test platforms give you per-question time data. Look for:
- **Questions you rushed (under 1 minute):** Did you make careless errors?
- **Questions you spent too long on (over 3 minutes):** Were they worth the time, or should you have guessed?
- **Section-level pacing:** Did you finish on time, or did you have to rush the last 5 questions?

### Step 6: Update Your Error Log (15 minutes)
Add every mistake from the mock to your error log. Tag each by type (conceptual, careless, time pressure, etc.) and topic. This is what you'll review before your next mock.

### Step 7: Adjust Your Study Plan (15 minutes)
Based on the mock, what should change in your study plan for the next 1-2 weeks?
- Which topics need more review?
- Which question types need more practice?
- Is your pacing strategy working?

## Reading Your Score Honestly

A mock score is one data point with a margin of error of roughly ±30 points. Here's how to interpret it:

### Single Mock: Not Much Signal
- One mock at 680 doesn't mean your "real" score is 680
- It means your current ability is somewhere in the 650-710 range, probably

### 3-4 Mocks: Real Signal
- Take the average of your last 3-4 mocks
- Look at the trend: improving, flat, or declining?
- The average minus 20-30 points is a rough estimate of your likely test-day score (since test-day pressure usually shaves off a bit)

### Score Plateaus
If your score stops improving despite continued study, you're in a plateau. Causes:
1. **You're practicing the wrong things.** You keep drilling topics you're already strong on.
2. **You're not analyzing mistakes deeply.** You're doing volume without reflection.
3. **Your pacing is broken.** You're leaving points on the table by rushing or timing out.
4. **You've hit a natural ceiling for your current strategy.** Time to upgrade your approach — more aggressive guessing on hard questions, better elimination techniques, etc.

## Common Mock Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Taking Too Many Mocks
Doing 10+ mocks doesn't help if you're not learning from each one. Quality > quantity.

### Mistake 2: Taking Mocks Too Close Together
Back-to-back mocks cause fatigue. Your second mock score will be artificially low, which damages your confidence.

### Mistake 3: Skipping the Debrief
This is the biggest mistake. The mock itself has limited value without analysis. A mock you don't debrief is essentially wasted.

### Mistake 4: Obsessing Over the Score
If you take a mock, look at the score, feel bad, and stop there — you learned nothing. The number is the least important output.

### Mistake 5: Taking Mocks When You're Not Ready
Some students take their first mock 3 days into prep. The score is meaningless because you haven't studied yet. Wait until you've covered at least 50% of the content before taking a full mock.

### Mistake 6: Taking Your Last Mock the Day Before the Test
Don't. Take your last mock 3-5 days before the test. The day before should be light review and rest — not a full-on exam.

## Mock Day Protocol

Treat each mock like a dress rehearsal for test day:

**The night before:**
- Get 7-8 hours of sleep
- No heavy studying in the evening
- Lay out clothes, water, snack

**Morning of:**
- Eat a normal breakfast (not too heavy)
- 15-20 minute warmup: a few easy practice questions to activate your brain
- Review your pacing strategy one last time

**During the mock:**
- Stick to your pacing plan
- Use the allowed break (10 minutes after the first section)
- If you're stuck on a question for more than 2:30, guess and move on — no hero plays

**After the mock:**
- Cool down — don't debrief immediately
- Plan when you'll debrief (aim for same day or next morning)
- Reward yourself — mocks are hard

---

## Key Takeaways

- A mock is a diagnostic, not just a score predictor
- 4-5 mocks spread across your prep is enough
- Simulate real conditions when taking a mock
- Spend 2-3 hours on the debrief — it's where the real learning happens
- Use the error log to track patterns across mocks
- Single mock scores are noisy — trust the trend over 3-4 mocks
