# Module 7: The Final Week

## The Counterintuitive Truth About the Final Week

If you have made it this far in the course, you have done the hard part. You have built the content knowledge, wired in the strategy, and logged the reps. The week before the GMAT is not about adding more. It is about arriving at the test center with a rested brain, a quiet nervous system, and full access to what you already know. Most students get this wrong. They feel the pressure ramping up, and they respond by studying more — longer sessions, extra mocks, new material — and they walk into the test depleted. Their prep was an 8 out of 10, and their execution on the day is a 6.

I want to be direct with you: more study in the final week does not mean more score. In fact, past a certain point, it means less. Cognition in the final seven days is fragile. Your working memory, your pattern recognition, and your ability to stay calm under time pressure all depend on sleep, recovery, and low cortisol. Every extra hour of grinding drains the tank you are supposed to show up with full.

The mental model you want is the athlete's taper. If you watch a marathoner in the week before a race, they are not running 20 miles a day. They are running short, easy sessions. They are eating well, sleeping long, and rehearsing the mental script of race day. The training load drops sharply, and the performance load stays high. They are not getting fitter in that week — they are converting fitness into readiness. That is exactly what you should be doing.

The final week is about three things, and only three things: peaking, rehearsing, and protecting. Peaking means aligning your physiology so that on test morning, your brain is the sharpest it has been in months. Rehearsing means running the test-day script until it is boring — wake time, breakfast, commute, warm-up, first question. Protecting means shielding yourself from the three things that sabotage most students at the finish line: new material, new habits, and other people's scores.

Your habits are now fixed. You will take the test with the toolkit you have, not a different one. Accept that. Your job is to show up and execute. A smart, deliberate taper can be worth 20 to 30 points on the day — not because you learn anything new, but because you access everything you already built.

## The 7-Day Taper Plan

Here is the day-by-day structure. Treat it as a scaffold, not a prison. If life intrudes on a particular day, shift, do not abandon. The sequencing matters more than the exact clock hours.

### Day -7: Last Full Mock, Under Full Simulation
- Take one official practice test, at the time of day your real test will start. Same start time, same break, same section order.
- Wear what you will wear. Eat what you will eat. Use only the scratch tools you will be given.
- Do not review the mock tonight. Get a solid dinner, log the raw score, go to bed on time.
- Rationale: this is your last real data point. You want it in real conditions so tomorrow's analysis is meaningful. Reviewing tired is worse than reviewing fresh.

### Day -6: Mock Analysis, No New Material
- Spend 60 to 90 minutes on the mock. Categorize every miss: content gap, strategy error, careless error, or timing error.
- Identify 2 to 3 patterns — not 10. If your DS on weighted averages broke twice, that is a pattern. If you missed one geometry question, that is noise.
- Write the patterns down. These are what you drill tomorrow. Everything else you leave alone.
- Rationale: you are compressing the mock into a short, specific to-do list. Breadth is your enemy this week.

### Day -5: Targeted Drill on Day -6 Patterns
- 1 to 2 hours max. Not 4. Not 6.
- Attack only the 2 to 3 patterns you identified. 10 to 15 problems per pattern is plenty.
- Start tapering caffeine. If you drink three cups, go to two. Caffeine creep over the week can wreck sleep on Day -1.
- Rationale: focused reps on a small set consolidate memory better than broad volume. You want precision, not tonnage.

### Day -4: Light Drill, Error Log Review, Logistics
- 45 to 60 minutes of review. Skim your error log from the past 30 days. Re-solve 5 to 10 questions that gave you trouble — pick ones you are now confident on, to reinforce.
- Confirm logistics: test center address, parking, ID you will bring, arrival time. If online, confirm your workstation meets requirements.
- Rationale: logistics handled early removes a background anxiety. Anxiety loves unresolved detail.

### Day -3: Formulas and Strategy Only
- No new problems today. Read your formula sheet, your pacing plan, your CR technique summaries, your DI playbook.
- If you are testing in person and the center is within reach, drive or walk the route. See the building. Know where to park.
- Rationale: the goal is calm familiarity. Your brain rehearses what you expose it to.

### Day -2: Full Dress Rehearsal of Test Morning
- Wake up at your test-day wake time. Eat your test-day breakfast. If you commute, commute.
- Run through your full warm-up: 15 minutes of light review, 2 to 3 easy practice questions.
- Then stop. The day is yours. Walk. Read something unrelated. Early dinner.
- Rationale: the body learns a sequence. When you repeat this on Day 0, it feels like the fifth time, not the first.

### Day -1: Rest
- No mocks. No hard problems. 30 minutes max of light review — formula sheet, pacing budgets, one or two strategy reminders.
- Go for a walk. Hydrate. Eat familiar food. Screens off by 9 pm.
- Pack your bag tonight, not tomorrow morning. ID, confirmation, snack, water, jacket.
- In bed at your normal bedtime. If you cannot sleep, that is fine — lying still in the dark is still restorative.
- Rationale: one poor night will not destroy your score. One poor week will. Protect the week.

### Day 0: Test Day
- Covered in detail below. Your job today is to execute the script you have already rehearsed.

## What to Review, What to Skip

The difference between a productive final week and a destructive one usually comes down to what students choose to look at. Below is the tight list. If it is not here, leave it alone.

### Review: Things That Must Be Automatic

- **Core formulas**: Pythagorean triples (3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17), quadratic formula, the distance-rate-time triangle, simple and compound interest, permutations and combinations, probability basics. If you have to think about these on test day, you lose seconds that compound.
- **Percent and fraction conversions**: 1/8 = 12.5%, 1/6 = 16.67%, 1/3 = 33.33%, 3/8 = 37.5%, 5/8 = 62.5%. These come up everywhere and you should not be dividing on scratch.
- **Exponent and root rules**: product rule, quotient rule, power rule, negative and fractional exponents.
- **Number properties rules**: divisibility rules for 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 11. Prime factorization habits. Even/odd algebra.
- **Your error log's repeating patterns**: the 3 to 5 traps that have caught you more than once. Read them aloud.
- **CR technique per question type**: one or two lines each — Strengthen, Weaken, Assumption, Inference, Boldface, Evaluate, Paradox. Know the move, not just the label.
- **Pacing budgets per section**: exact time checkpoints. For Quant: where you should be at question 7, 14, 21. For Verbal and DI: the same.

### Review: Confidence Fuel

- 15 to 20 minutes of questions you have recently nailed. Yes — questions you got right. Your brain remembers the last few reps most vividly. Let those last reps be wins.
- A highlight reel of your best moments from prep: a mock score you are proud of, a topic you turned around, a hard problem you cracked. This is not vanity. It is calibration.

### Skip: Everything Else

- **New content you have not mastered.** If you have not nailed combinatorics by Day -7, you are not going to nail it by Day -1. Trying to stuff it in will not help you on that one question, and it will rattle your confidence across the other 63.
- **Full-length mocks after Day -7.** Diminishing returns. Mocks are 3+ hours of cognitive load plus hours of analysis. The fatigue cost is far greater than the information gain this close to the day.
- **Friends' scores, Reddit threads, Discord brags, YouTube GMAT content.** None of it is about you. All of it is noise. Someone scored 775 on their first try and posted about it. Good for them. It has zero bearing on your test on Saturday.
- **New strategies or new techniques.** Do not change your CR approach, your pacing plan, or your guessing protocol in the final week. Whatever you have is what you use.
- **Caffeine, supplement, or sleep-aid experiments.** Do not start a new routine this week. Whatever your body is used to is what you use.

One more thing. If something in this list makes you anxious because you "haven't covered it enough" — that is a feeling, not a fact. You have covered it. Trust the work.

## The Mental Game in the Final Week

Anxiety in the final week is not a sign something is wrong. It is a sign you care. Champions feel it too. The goal is not to eliminate the anxiety — it is to route it so it does not hijack your performance.

### Expect It and Plan For It

The night you feel the most nervous is often Day -3 or Day -2. The mind, having finally let go of the grind, starts to catastrophize. "What if I freeze?" "What if I blank on quant?" "What if I wake up sick?" This is normal. Name it as the pre-fight jitters and keep moving through the plan. The anxiety does not mean you are unprepared. It means the test matters to you.

### Box Breathing and the 4-7-8 Pattern

Build a 5-minute morning practice starting Day -7. Sit quietly, eyes closed. Inhale for 4 seconds, hold for 7, exhale for 8. Repeat 6 to 8 cycles. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system, drops your heart rate, and gives you a tool you can deploy in 60 seconds on test day when you feel anxiety spike. A tool practiced is a tool you can actually use. A tool you read about once is nothing.

### Visualization

Two or three times during the week, spend 10 minutes mentally rehearsing the sequence: waking up, eating breakfast, arriving at the center, sitting down at the workstation, reading the first question, solving it calmly. Then visualize hitting a hard question you do not see a path on — and instead of panicking, you take a breath, eliminate two choices, guess, flag, and move on. You recover. Visualization is not magic; it is pre-loading the response pattern so that when the moment comes, it feels rehearsed rather than novel.

### Sleep Protocol

This is non-negotiable. Aim for 8 hours every night of the taper. Same bedtime, same wake time. Screens off 60 minutes before bed — even a few minutes of phone scrolling in bed elevates cortisol and fragments sleep. If you have trouble falling asleep, do not lie there frustrated. Get up, read something boring under dim light for 15 minutes, then try again. Lying awake in the dark also counts as rest for the body.

### Confidence Maintenance

Spend 20 minutes once this week reviewing a set of problems you crushed. Pick hard ones you solved well. Let your brain feel competent. Athletes watch tape of themselves winning before big games for exactly this reason.

### The Worst-Case Plan

Here is the release valve most students miss. Write down, on paper, what you will do if the test goes badly. Something like: "If I score below my target, I will take 48 hours off, review what happened, and register for a retake 4 to 6 weeks out." The GMAT Focus allows up to 5 attempts per 12-month period. There is a net under you. Knowing the net is there lets you perform free. The students who white-knuckle the test because "this is my only shot" typically underperform. Make it not your only shot in your mind, even if you only plan to take it once.

## Logistics Checklist

Logistics is where a great prep gets ambushed. Handle it now, not on test morning.

### One Week Out (Day -7 or -6)

- **Confirm your test time and location** through your mba.com account. Re-read the confirmation email end to end.
- **Map the route.** Know the parking situation. Note whether the building has security screening that might add time.
- **Arrival time**: plan to arrive 30 minutes before your appointment. Test centers want you there early anyway, and buffer absorbs traffic surprises.
- **IDs**: two forms are typically required. Your primary must be a government-issued photo ID with a signature. The name on the ID must match your registration exactly — middle name, hyphens, all of it. If it does not match, fix it now, not on the day.

### Clothing

- Dress comfortable and layered. Test centers run cold in summer and hot in winter — you will not know until you sit down. A light jacket or hoodie over a t-shirt gives you options.
- Closed-toe shoes. Some centers require them.
- Nothing noisy. Avoid jewelry that jingles, stiff jeans, collars that itch.

### What to Bring

- Primary ID and a secondary ID.
- Printed confirmation email — not just on your phone. Your phone will be locked up.
- Water bottle and a snack (banana, granola bar, nuts) for the optional break.
- A light jacket.
- Reading glasses if you wear them.
- Keys, wallet, phone — these will go in a locker.

### The Home/Travel Plan

- If you have not walked the route, do it. See the door. See the check-in desk if you can.
- Know your backup: if your car fails, what is the taxi number? What is the public transit option?
- Weather check Day -1. Leave earlier if there is rain or snow.

### Online-Test Tech

If you are taking the online version, the tech is your biggest risk.

- **3 days before**, run the system check from the GMAT portal. Test your webcam, microphone, and internet.
- **Night before**, run it again. Close every other app. Restart the computer. Confirm ethernet if possible, or at minimum a strong Wi-Fi signal.
- Clear your workspace per the requirements: no notes on walls, no extra monitors, no paper (you get a digital whiteboard), no phone in the room, nothing within reach that is not the provided tools.
- Have a second laptop as a backup if you can. Technical failures on the day can sometimes be salvaged by switching machines.

## The Night Before

Tonight is about calm, not preparation. The preparation is done.

- **Dinner**: eat something familiar. This is not the night to try sushi for the first time. A meal you have eaten dozens of times, moderate size, not too heavy, not too spicy. Finish 3 hours before bed.
- **Pack your bag now.** Do not leave this for the morning. IDs, confirmation printout, water bottle, snack, light jacket, keys, wallet. The test center provides the laminated yellow note boards and markers — do not bring your own.
- **Light review only.** 30 minutes maximum. Skim your formula sheet. Re-read your pacing plan. Read the two or three CR summaries you trust. That is it.
- **No mock exams tonight. No new problems.** I do not care how you feel. Do not do it. You will not learn anything you will remember by the morning, and you risk rattling yourself over a stray mistake.
- **Sleep routine**: same bedtime you have been keeping all week. Dim lights an hour before. No screens in bed.
- **Alarm plus backup.** Set your phone alarm. Set a second alarm — a secondary phone, a bedside clock, or ask a household member to check on you. The cost of oversleeping is catastrophic; the cost of redundancy is zero.
- **If you cannot sleep**: do not panic. One suboptimal night, after a week of good sleep, is survivable. Read something dull, stay in bed, rest the body even if the mind is busy.

## Test-Day Morning Protocol

Execute the script. You have practiced it on Day -2. Today is the fifth or sixth time you have run this sequence.

### Wake 3 Hours Before Test Start

Your brain needs about 90 to 120 minutes after waking to be fully online. If your test starts at 10 am, wake at 7. If it starts at 8 am, wake at 5. Yes, early. Do not try to nap your way into sharpness — it does not work. Wake, stay up, and get going.

### Shower and Light Exposure

Cool-to-warm shower. Open the blinds. Get light on your face. Light is the strongest signal to your circadian system that it is go-time.

### Breakfast

Eat exactly what you tested during mocks. No experiments. For most people this is something like: oatmeal with fruit, or eggs with toast, or a bagel with peanut butter. Moderate protein, moderate complex carbs, not too much fat. Coffee at your usual level — not more. Hydrate, but do not overdo it. You do not want to be mid-Quant-section wondering if you can hold it.

### Light Movement

10 to 20 minutes of light exercise, or a 20-minute relaxed walk. The goal is to raise your heart rate gently, increase blood flow to the brain, and discharge some adrenaline. Do not go hard — no intervals, no heavy lifting. A brisk walk is the gold standard.

### The Warm-Up

15 minutes. 2 to 3 easy practice questions — emphasis on easy. You are priming the brain, not testing it. Do not pick your weakest area. Do not pick hard problems. Pick things you will get right, so your last experience before walking in is a win. Read one CR stimulus, solve one Quant Problem Solving, look at one DI table. Short. Confident. Done.

### Leave Early

30 to 45 minutes of buffer. If you arrive with 20 minutes to spare, you will be calm. If you arrive with 2 minutes to spare, you are fighting your nervous system for the first 15 questions.

### At the Center

Check in. Use the bathroom. Store your belongings. Sit down at the workstation. Adjust the chair. Take three slow breaths. Remind yourself of your start phrase — one short sentence you have rehearsed, like "I prepared. I'm here. Let's work."

## During the Test

The test is not the time to think about strategy. The test is the time to execute the strategy you already built. What follows are the few in-test habits that separate students who convert prep into score from those who do not.

### Section 1: Settle, Do Not Sprint

The first 3 to 5 questions set the tone for the whole section adaptively and psychologically. Do not rush them. Read each stem fully before scanning answers. Lock in the question type. Solve deliberately. If question 1 takes you 2:30 and it is correct, that is a win, not an overspend. You are buying calibration.

### Pacing Checks

Glance at the clock every 5 questions, not every question. Constant clock-checking is a stress multiplier and it does not help your pacing. Know your checkpoints:

- If you are ahead, do not speed up. Bank the time for the hard question that is coming.
- If you are behind by less than 2 minutes, stay steady. You will claw it back.
- If you are behind by more than 2 minutes, pick the next stuck-feeling question and flag it early. Do not drain the clock.

### The 90-Second Rule on Hard Questions

When you hit a question you do not see a path on: give yourself 90 seconds of real effort. Read it fully. Try a setup. Try an alternative — plug in numbers, back-solve from answers, estimate. If at 90 seconds you still do not have a foothold: eliminate what you can, guess, flag if flagging is available, move on. Do not let one question eat 3 or 4 minutes. That single indulgence costs you 2 or 3 later questions you would have gotten right.

### The Break

You get an optional break between sections. Take it. Use it deliberately.

- Bathroom first, even if you do not feel you need to. It resets you physically.
- Water. A few sips of snack — banana, small granola bar. Nothing heavy.
- 30 seconds of box breathing. Drop your shoulders.
- **Do not talk to other test-takers about the section.** Do not discuss questions. Do not compare. What you just did is done. Hearing "oh, question 12 was crazy" can unravel you.

### Mental Reset Between Sections

Before the next section starts, give yourself 30 seconds of intentional reset. Deep breaths. Remind yourself: "New section. Blank slate. First question." The previous section is locked. You cannot change it, so stop spending any cognition on it.

### Stay Present

You will miss some questions. Everyone does. The students who lose 40 points in one section are usually the ones who obsess over a question they know they botched, then carry that obsession into the next five. The math on this is brutal: you cannot take the missed question back, and you can lose five more by spiraling. The mantra is: next question, fresh start. Every single time.

## After the Test

You are going to finish the test and feel one of three things: elated, crushed, or numb. All three are normal. None of them are reliable signals about your actual score.

- **Write down your unofficial score** the moment it appears on screen. Screenshot if you can.
- **Take a 48-hour break from GMAT thinking**, regardless of outcome. No reviewing, no score calculators, no Reddit threads analyzing other people's days. Let your nervous system come down.
- **If you hit your target**: celebrate. Take a proper break. Turn your attention to applications — essays, recommenders, school selection. This is the next thing, and it is a full job on its own.
- **If you missed it**: breathe. You have options. The GMAT Focus permits up to 5 attempts per 12-month period. Before scheduling a retake, do the diagnostic: what was the gap between your last mock score and your actual score? If the gap is large, the issue was usually nerves, sleep, or pacing under real stakes — all fixable. If the gap is small, the issue is content or strategy — also fixable, but with a different plan. Do not schedule a retake in the first 48 hours. Let the data settle.

## This Week's Homework

- **Calendar the 7-day taper** now. Block each day in your calendar with the exact tasks from the plan above. Do not leave any day as "I'll figure it out."
- **Handle logistics**: confirm test center, print confirmation, check IDs, plan route, pack your bag tonight even though the test is still days away. Nothing to worry about later.
- **Pick your test-day breakfast** and eat it tomorrow morning as a test run. If it sits well, it is the one. If not, try the backup.
- **Pick your start phrase** — the short sentence you will say to yourself before question 1. Write it down. Use it in every mock and drill from now until test day.
- **Put this module on your phone** or print the taper plan. On Day -5 you will be foggy, and you do not want to be re-deriving the schedule. You want to be executing it.

You prepared. You did the reps. The final week is not more prep — it is conversion. Convert what you built into what shows up on the screen. See you in Module 8.
