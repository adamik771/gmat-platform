# Module 7: The Final Week

## Learning Objectives
- Build a final week study plan that peaks your performance on test day
- Understand what NOT to do in the last 7 days (this is harder than it sounds)
- Manage test-day anxiety with proven techniques
- Execute a flawless test day from wake-up to walkout

---

## The Final Week Mindset

The last 7 days are not about learning new material. They're about three things:
1. **Confidence:** Reinforce what you already know
2. **Peaking:** Arriving at test day fresh, not exhausted
3. **Execution:** Tightening your pacing and test-day routine

If you're trying to learn something completely new in the last week, you're making a mistake. At this point, your score is largely determined by what you already know. Your job is to bring your A-game, not to add new knowledge.

---

## The Final Week Study Plan

### Day 7 (1 week out): Light Diagnostic
- Take a short practice set (20-30 questions) across all sections
- Identify 2-3 topics that feel shaky
- Do NOT take a full mock — you need to save your energy

### Day 6-5: Targeted Review
- Focus on the 2-3 shaky topics from Day 7
- Review the relevant lessons
- Do 20-30 practice questions per topic
- Review your error log — look for patterns from the last 2 weeks

### Day 4: Final Mock (Optional)
- If you want one last data point, take a mock today — NOT later
- Take it in the morning at the time you'll take the real test
- Do a 1-hour debrief (not a full 3-hour one) — focus on big-picture patterns, not every single mistake

### Day 3: Error Log Deep Dive
- Read through your entire error log from the beginning
- Re-do 20-30 questions you got wrong in the past
- If you still get them wrong, this is a content gap to drill specifically tomorrow
- No mock today — let yesterday's fatigue dissipate

### Day 2: Light Review
- 1-2 hours of review max
- Focus on your strongest topics (confidence building)
- Review the formula sheet and strategy guides
- Go to bed early

### Day 1 (day before the test): Rest Mode
- NO full practice sets
- 15-30 minutes of light review ONLY (skim the formula sheet, re-read one or two lessons)
- Spend the day relaxing: go for a walk, watch a movie, have a good meal
- Prepare everything for tomorrow (see checklist below)
- Bed by 10pm

### Day 0 (test day): Execution Mode
- Wake up 2-3 hours before your test time
- Normal breakfast (not too heavy)
- 10-15 minute warmup with easy practice questions (NOT hard ones)
- Leave with plenty of buffer time to reach the test center or log in to the online test

## What NOT to Do in the Final Week

### 1. Don't Try to Learn New Topics
If you haven't mastered a topic by Day 7, you probably won't master it in the last week. Focus on what you know, not what you don't.

### 2. Don't Take Back-to-Back Mocks
Mocks are exhausting. Taking multiple mocks in the final week leads to burnout on test day. Max one mock in the last 5 days.

### 3. Don't Change Your Strategy
The final week is not the time to switch to a new approach for Critical Reasoning or a new pacing strategy. Your brain needs consistency to perform under pressure.

### 4. Don't Compare Yourself to Others
Reddit threads, Discord channels, and prep forums are full of people saying they scored 750 on their first try. Ignore them. Your journey is yours.

### 5. Don't Cram the Night Before
A cram session the night before does more harm than good. You'll sleep worse, feel more anxious, and arrive exhausted. Trust that you know what you know.

### 6. Don't Drink Alcohol in the Final 3 Days
Alcohol disrupts sleep quality for up to 72 hours. Even one drink two nights before can impact cognitive performance.

### 7. Don't Skip the Warmup on Test Day
Going in cold is a mistake. Do 10-15 minutes of easy practice questions before the test to activate your brain.

## Managing Test-Day Anxiety

Almost everyone feels anxious on test day. The goal isn't to eliminate the anxiety — it's to manage it.

### Technique 1: Reframe the Nerves
Nervousness and excitement are physiologically identical — racing heart, shallow breathing, alertness. The only difference is the label you apply. Instead of thinking "I'm nervous," think "I'm ready. My body is preparing me for performance."

### Technique 2: Box Breathing
When you feel overwhelmed, use box breathing:
1. Inhale for 4 seconds
2. Hold for 4 seconds
3. Exhale for 4 seconds
4. Hold for 4 seconds
5. Repeat 4-5 times

This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and calms your heart rate in under a minute.

### Technique 3: The Pre-Exam Ritual
Create a small ritual you do before every mock and before the real test:
- Same breakfast
- Same warmup routine
- Same "start phrase" you tell yourself (e.g., "I've prepared for this. Let's do the work.")

Rituals reduce anxiety by creating predictability in an unpredictable situation.

### Technique 4: Dealing with a Bad Question
Everyone encounters a question they can't solve. Don't panic. The GMAT is adaptive, but one missed question won't destroy your score. Make your best guess, move on, and forget it. Anxiety over one question poisons the next five.

### Technique 5: The Break Reset
Use your break between sections (after the first section) strategically:
- Don't think about how the previous section went
- Eat a small snack (a banana, a granola bar — nothing heavy)
- Hydrate
- Do 2-3 slow deep breaths
- Tell yourself: "New section, fresh start."

## Test Day Execution

### The Morning Of

**2-3 hours before the test:**
- Wake up, shower, get dressed
- Eat breakfast (something you've eaten many times before — NOT a new "brain food" experiment)
- Drink water, not too much caffeine (stick to your normal level)

**1 hour before:**
- Leave early for the test center, or log in early for the online test
- Bring: ID, confirmation email, a snack, water, a light jacket (test centers are often cold)

**15 minutes before:**
- Do a light warmup (3-5 easy questions)
- Box breathe for 1-2 minutes
- Visualize starting the test calmly and confidently

### During the Test

**First section (45 minutes):**
- Read each question carefully — don't rush the first few
- Stick to your pacing plan
- If you're stuck, guess and move on (no hero plays)

**Break (10 minutes):**
- Eat a snack
- Hydrate
- Bathroom break if needed
- DO NOT review the previous section mentally — it's done
- Reset mentally: "Next section, fresh start."

**Remaining sections (45 minutes each):**
- Apply the same pacing discipline
- Trust your preparation
- If anxiety spikes, use box breathing for 20-30 seconds

### After the Test

**Immediately after:**
- Whatever the score, you did the work
- Don't obsess over specific questions — you'll drive yourself crazy trying to remember them
- Reward yourself — you earned it

**If your score is lower than expected:**
- Take 24-48 hours before making any decisions
- Evaluate: was it a content gap, a pacing issue, or just a bad day?
- A retake is always an option — the GMAT Focus allows up to 5 attempts in a 12-month period

**If your score is where you wanted:**
- Celebrate
- Start your application immediately — this is usually the next thing to stress about

## Test Day Checklist

### The Night Before
- [ ] Confirm test time and location (or online platform login)
- [ ] Charge your laptop (for online tests)
- [ ] Lay out clothes for tomorrow (comfortable, layers)
- [ ] Pack bag: ID, confirmation, snack, water, light jacket, tissues
- [ ] Set 2 alarms
- [ ] Light review only — no cramming
- [ ] Bed by 10pm

### The Morning Of
- [ ] Wake up 2-3 hours before test time
- [ ] Eat normal breakfast
- [ ] Shower and get dressed
- [ ] Review formula sheet briefly (10 min max)
- [ ] Do 5-10 easy warmup questions
- [ ] Leave with 45+ minutes of buffer
- [ ] Box breathe 1-2 minutes before starting

### Right Before the Test
- [ ] Bathroom break
- [ ] Sip of water
- [ ] Positive self-talk: "I've prepared. I'm ready. Let's do the work."

---

## Key Takeaways

- The final week is about confidence and execution, not new learning
- Rest and sleep matter more than one more practice set
- Develop a pre-exam ritual to manage anxiety
- Use box breathing when anxiety spikes
- One bad question doesn't define your score — move on quickly
- Execute a consistent test-day routine
